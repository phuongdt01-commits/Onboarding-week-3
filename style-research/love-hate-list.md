# My Writing Love List & Hate List

---

## Phrases and Words I Love

**Conversational openers and transitions:**
- "Here's the thing" / "Here's why this matters"
- "Let's get something straight"
- "But hey, don't take my word for it"
- Starting sentences with "But" or "And"
- "In practice" / "The real question is"
- "That's actually good news"

**Short, complete sentences that land a single point:**
- "You don't research, stock, or ship these products. You just earn commission when customers buy them."
- "If you get it wrong, you'll feel it quickly in backlogs and SLAs."
- "A mention isn't a recommendation, but it still matters."
- "AI acts as a filter between buyers and brands."
- "Depth gets cited. Surface-level content gets ignored."

**Honest, self-aware lines:**
- "I rarely click them myself"
- Concessions like "results fell short" before reframing
- Naming what goes wrong, not just what goes right
- Parenthetical asides that feel like peer-to-peer tips: "(and communicate up)" or "(at least)"

**Reframing lines that shift how you think:**
- "Creating a store is fairly easy. Selling isn't."
- "You're not chasing traffic. You're choosing relevance over reach."
- The "old way vs. new way" flip: here's what you assumed, here's why it's wrong now

**Action-oriented, direct language:**
- "In this post I show you how to..."
- "Trim the fluff, get straight to the point"
- Simple verbs: show, prove, test, build, earn, pick

**Concrete micro-scenarios instead of abstract claims:**
- "Rainbow shoelaces to go with new sneakers" instead of "complementary product offerings"
- "Selling a banjo or ukulele instead of a generic guitar" instead of "find a niche"
- "Think about your last busy shopping weekend. How many customers searched for something you didn't have?"

## Phrases and Words I Hate

**Corporate filler that sounds important but says nothing:**
- "Robust governance and fast innovation can coexist"
- "Compounds value" / "Broader AI transformation story"
- "High-performing support organization"
- "Continued leadership in shaping responsible AI practices globally"
- "Low-risk, clever strategy to unlock concealed profits"
- "Complementary product offerings" (just say "rainbow shoelaces")

**Buzzwords and AI-speak:**
- "Leverage" / "Utilize" / "Streamlined"
- "Game-changer" / "Unlock the power of" / "Dive deep"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "It's important to note" / "It should be noted" / "In conclusion"
- "Powerful, robust, comprehensive" stacked together

**Vague motivational filler:**
- "Your collaborative advantage"
- "Shift from competition to collaboration"
- "Making the pie bigger for everyone"
- "Set achievable and measurable goals"

**Paraphrasing-tool artifacts:**
- "Transforms the post-purchase interaction into the possibility to promote income"
- "Extra value deals ensure the customer that they have made the correct decision"
- Anything that reads like someone ran a clear sentence through a thesaurus

**Copywriting tricks pretending to be writing:**
- Fragment sentences used for fake emphasis: "Simple. Scalable. And surprisingly effective." These feel like ad copy, not real prose.
- Overusing one-word paragraphs or dramatic line breaks as a substitute for actual pacing

## Formatting Preferences

**Bullet points and numbered lists:**
- Use sparingly. Bullets work for genuinely scannable items: checklists, feature comparisons, quick specs. But ideas that need explanation should live in paragraphs with connective tissue between them. A bullet wall feels "like a skeleton without muscle."
- Numbered steps work well inside how-to sections (Step 1 through Step 9), but the whole article shouldn't feel like a listicle.

**Bold text:**
- Fine for key terms or visual anchors in long sections, but not every paragraph needs a bolded phrase. One bold per section max, or skip it entirely.

**Em dashes:**
- No. Replace with commas, periods, or colons. Or just rewrite the sentence.

**One-sentence paragraphs for impact:**
- Yes, but only with complete sentences. Not fragments. A standalone paragraph like "For AI visibility, silence isn't neutral" works because it's a full thought that lands before the next idea starts. Two or three per article max.

**Block quotes:**
- Only for real customer quotes or data callouts. Never for decorative pull quotes that repeat something already said in the body.

**Tables:**
- Love comparison tables for pricing, features, or tool comparisons. But "How it works" steps should not live in table format. That's documentation, not storytelling.

**White space:**
- Heavy. Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences). But the ideas between those paragraphs need to connect. Not a bullet list. Not a wall. A rhythm.

## Opening Style

**What I want:** Start with the problem, the constraint, or the twist. Not with context. Not with definitions. Not with "AI is changing everything."

**Patterns I love:**
- **Problem-first:** Name the pain before the solution. "Every merchant knows this story: A customer lands on your store, searches for exactly what they want, but you don't carry it."
- **Constraint-first:** Lead with what makes this harder than people think. "Fintech in AI search plays by much stricter rules."
- **Reframe-first:** Flip the reader's assumption in the first two lines. "Creating a store is fairly easy. Selling isn't."
- **Contrarian hook:** Call out bad advice, then present your angle. The Ahrefs piece opens by debunking a Reddit study before sharing their own data.
- **Concede-then-reframe:** Acknowledge a weakness honestly, then immediately offer context that changes how you see it.

**What I don't want:**
- Throat-clearing intros
- Stats without stakes
- "In this article, we'll explore..."
- Leading with definitions of things the reader already knows

---

## Closing Style

**What I want:** A strong closing thought that adds something new or reframes what the reader just learned. Not a summary. Not a bulleted recap. Not a pep talk.

**What I hate (and almost every article I analyzed does this):**
- Conclusions that repeat section headers in bullet form
- Generic sign-offs: "Now dive deeper" / "Set your team up for success"
- Corporate boilerplate: "Our promise is simple: to build AI that is not only powerful and efficient, but safe, transparent..."
- Conclusions that are really just FAQs or checklists
- Endings that refuse to commit to a takeaway

**What I'd prefer instead:**
- A single memorable line that reframes the whole piece
- A forward-looking question the reader takes with them
- A callback to the opening tension that closes the loop
- An honest "here's what I'd do" recommendation

**Honest note:** I haven't found a closing I truly love yet across these 12 articles. Most content writers are bad at endings. That's an opportunity to stand out.

## Transition Style

**What I like:**
- **Questions as soft resets** between sections: "So what is a co-citation?" or "Here's why this matters:"
- **Short bridge sentences** at the end of a section that pull you into the next one: "Once you've found your niche and decided what to sell, it's time to think about where to get your inventory from."
- **"Old world vs. new world" framing** as a transition device: here's what you assumed, here's why it's wrong now
- **Concrete before abstract.** Show me the shoelaces and water bottles before you tell me about "collaborative advantage."
- **Proof first, explanation second.** Lead with the screenshot or real search result, then explain why it matters.

**What I don't like:**
- Mid-article CTA blocks that break reading momentum
- "Series callout" boxes inserted right when the intro is building energy
- Repeating the same transition pattern more than twice in one article
- Using "This means that" or "This allows you to" as connectors

## Things AI Does That I Want to Stop

**Sentence-level problems:**
- **Stacking adjectives:** "Powerful, robust, comprehensive solution" or "seamless, intuitive, and user-friendly experience." Pick one word. If you can't pick one, the sentence is too vague.
- **Fragment sentences for fake drama:** "Simple. Scalable. And surprisingly effective." Every sentence needs a subject, a verb, and a clear point.
- **Hedging everything:** "It might be worth considering..." / "This could potentially help..." Just say it. Be opinionated. Defend the stance with proof.
- **Fake enthusiasm:** "This is a really exciting development" or "The possibilities are truly endless." If the thing is exciting, show me why. Don't tell me how to feel.

**Structural problems:**
- **Opening paragraphs that explain what the article will cover instead of hooking the reader.** "In this article, we'll explore five strategies for..." No. Start with the problem or the twist.
- **Conclusions that summarize instead of closing.** Don't give me "In summary, we covered X, Y, and Z." Give me one sharp thought that sticks.
- **Giving equal weight to every point.** Real writing has hierarchy. Some things get one sentence. Some get a full section. Know the difference.
- **Defaulting to bullets when prose would be better.** If an idea needs explanation, write it out. Connect it to the idea before and after it.

**Voice problems:**
- **Disclaimer padding:** "It's worth noting that..." / "It's important to understand that..." / "While there are many factors to consider..." Cut all of these. Say the thing.
- **Every paragraph starting the same way.** AI loves starting 3-4 paragraphs in a row with "The" or "This" or "When it comes to." Vary the rhythm.
- **False balance instead of having a point of view.** Don't give equal space to everything just to seem objective. Take a stance. Back it up. Be honest about tradeoffs. But commit.
- **Talking down to the reader.** Skip basic definitions the reader already knows. Use parenthetical asides instead of formal explanations. Write like you're one step ahead, not ten steps above.

## My North Star (from Style Compass)

**Tone:** Conversational-professional. A smart person talking to another smart person.

**Sentences:** Short and complete as the default. Every sentence has a subject, a verb, and a clear point.

**Evidence:** Examples first, data as backup. Numbers prove. Examples persuade.

**Voice:** Opinionated with evidence. Take a stance, defend it with proof, be honest about tradeoffs.

**Pacing:** Fast by default with deliberate slowdowns for key ideas. Not a bullet list. Not a wall. A rhythm.

**Reader relationship:** Peer explaining alongside. Honest concessions. Parenthetical asides. Never talking down.
