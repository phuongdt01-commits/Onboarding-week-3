# My Style Compass

---

## Tone Spectrum

Casual --------**X**---------- Formal

**My preference:** Conversational-professional. Closer to casual, but never sloppy.

**Why:** Across every article I reviewed, I consistently praised first-person voice ("In my testing...", "I've found..."), honest asides, and peer-to-peer tone. I flagged corporate boilerplate every single time it appeared. Phrases like "robust governance and fast innovation can coexist" and "continued leadership in shaping responsible AI practices globally" always landed in my "didn't like" column. But I also never praised anything that felt unserious or too informal. The sweet spot is a smart person talking to another smart person. Professional context, human delivery.

**Example of what this sounds like:**
> "What I like most about ChatGPT is its versatility - it rarely fails completely on any task, making it a solid all-around choice." (EComposer)

---

## Sentence Length

Short & punchy ------**X**-------- Long & flowing

**My preference:** Short and clear as the default, with occasional longer sentences when the idea needs room. Every sentence should be complete, not a fragment.

**Why:** I praised short paragraphs (1-3 sentences) in every article I reviewed. I flagged dense paragraphs and long table cells as negatives. But "short" doesn't mean broken. Fragment sentences like "Simple. Scalable. And surprisingly effective." feel like copywriting tricks, not real writing. The sentences I actually wanted to steal were all grammatically complete. They just didn't waste words. A good short sentence has a subject, a verb, and a clear point. It earns its period by finishing a thought, not by chopping one in half.

**Examples:**
> "You don't research, stock, or ship these products. You just earn commission when customers buy them." (Shopify) — Two short sentences. Both complete. Both crystal clear.

> "If you get it wrong, you'll feel it quickly in backlogs and SLAs." (Intercom) — One sentence, real stakes, no filler.

> "A mention isn't a recommendation, but it still matters." (Backlinko fintech) — Short, complete, and the comma does real structural work.

---

## Vocabulary

Simple & accessible ------**X**-------- Technical & precise

**My preference:** Simple by default. Technical only when precision changes meaning.

**Why:** I flagged "high-performing support organization," "compounds value," and "broader AI transformation story" as corporate fluff. I praised "Not just any links. The kind that actually moves the needle." I liked when writers said "rainbow shoelaces" instead of "complementary product offerings." But I also valued specific numbers (53.4%, $60-$120 billion, 88.7% accuracy rate) and precise terminology when it earned its place. Simple words, specific data.

**Example:**
> "You don't research, stock, or ship these products. You just earn commission when customers buy them." (Shopify)

---

## Personality

Neutral & objective ----------**X**---- Opinionated & personal

**My preference:** Opinionated with evidence. Strong lean toward personal voice.

**Why:** I praised every author who took a clear stance: calling out bad Reddit research (Ahrefs), admitting "I rarely click citations myself" (Backlinko fintech), choosing a side while staying honest about tradeoffs. I flagged false balance as a weakness (EComposer giving equal space to all three chatbots). I also repeatedly flagged weak conclusions that refused to commit to a takeaway. I want the writer to have a point of view and defend it with proof.

**Example:**
> "It's tempting to be conservative and cap your automation assumptions at 40-50% 'because AI is new.'" (Intercom) — the quote marks around "because AI is new" do the opinion work without being preachy.

---

## Structure

Dense paragraphs ----------------**X** Lots of white space

**My preference:** Heavy white space. Short paragraphs. But connected prose, not just bullet walls.

**Why:** I praised short paragraphs in every single review. I also flagged excessive bullet points as a negative twice (Intercom capacity planning and EcomRise upsell article). The EcomRise piece got called out specifically: "Almost every section defaults to bullets instead of explaining ideas in connected prose... It makes the article feel shallow, like a skeleton without muscle." White space is essential, but ideas need connective tissue between them.

**Example:**
> Backlinko's pattern of one-sentence paragraph as emphasis beat, then 2-3 sentence explanation, then another beat. Not a bullet list. Not a wall. A rhythm.

---

## Humor

Serious --------**X**---------- Playful

**My preference:** Dry and occasional. Never the point, always a tool.

**Why:** I flagged conversational asides like "But hey, don't take my word for it" (Ahrefs) as positives. I liked the Shopify "fries with that" analogy. But I never praised an article for being funny. Humor shows up as a pacing tool: a quick aside that keeps the reader awake between dense sections. It should feel like the writer's natural voice leaking through, not a performance.

**Example:**
> "Not exactly the 10,000+ word mega-guides some are recommending." (Ahrefs) — understated, does its job, moves on.

---

## Evidence Style

Stories & examples ---**X**----------- Data & statistics

**My preference:** Examples first, data as backup. Strong lean toward concrete scenarios.

**Why:** The moments I praised most across all articles were concrete micro-scenarios: selling a banjo instead of a guitar (Style Factory), rainbow shoelaces with sneakers (Shopify), the UnderFit x Black Lapel story with 150+ sales (Backlinko). I also valued specific data (53.4% of cited pages under 1,000 words, $60-$120B in healthcare savings). But I never praised an article for data alone. Every stat I highlighted came with context. Numbers prove. Examples persuade.

**Example:**
> "Think about your last busy shopping weekend. How many customers searched for something you didn't have?" (Shopify) — no data needed. The scenario does the work.

---

## Reader Relationship

Expert talking down ----------------**X** Friend explaining alongside

**My preference:** Peer explaining alongside. Strong lean toward "friend" end.

**Why:** I consistently praised parenthetical asides ("and communicate up"), honest concessions ("I rarely click them myself"), and phrases like "I keep all three bookmarked." I flagged every instance of talking-down language: "it should be noted," credentials-heavy conclusions, and phrasing that assumed the reader needed basic definitions they clearly didn't (the "What are AI chatbots?" section). The best articles I reviewed felt like getting advice from someone one step ahead, not ten steps above.

**Example:**
> "And while it might feel strange to lower these, just remember (and communicate up) that it's not because people are working less, they're working differently." (Intercom)

---

## Pacing

Steady & even ----------------**X** Varies (fast sections + slow deep dives)

**My preference:** Varied. Fast by default with deliberate slowdowns for key ideas.

**Why:** I praised short standalone paragraphs that let a key point breathe before the next idea starts. I also liked the proof-first structure where screenshots come before explanation. And I liked when articles slowed down for the strongest example (the healthcare savings breakdown in Forbes, the UnderFit case study in Backlinko). Steady pacing got flagged as a weakness: "reads like a checklist doc instead of a blog post" (Intercom capacity planning). The rhythm I prefer is: make a clear point in one or two sentences, let it land, then build on it.

**Examples:**
> **Fast:** "You don't research, stock, or ship these products. You just earn commission when customers buy them." Two sentences, done, next section.

> **Slow:** The full breakdown of how Descript's niche messaging shows up in ChatGPT results, with screenshots and step-by-step analysis.

---

## Summary: My Writing DNA

Based on these 12 articles, here's the pattern:

**I gravitate toward writing that is:** conversational but credible, opinionated but evidence-backed, short in rhythm but deep in substance.

**My biggest pet peeves are:** corporate filler language, weak conclusions that just summarize, bullet-point walls without connecting prose, and false balance that avoids taking a stance.

**The techniques I keep flagging as "steal-worthy":**
- Short standalone paragraphs that let a key point land before moving on
- Questions as transitions between sections
- Concrete micro-scenarios before abstract principles
- Honest concessions that build trust ("this doesn't always work because...")
- The "old way vs. new way" reframing pattern
- Proof first, explanation second

**My north star sentence style:**
Direct. Complete. Says one thing clearly, then stops. Every sentence earns its period by finishing a thought, not by chopping one in half.
