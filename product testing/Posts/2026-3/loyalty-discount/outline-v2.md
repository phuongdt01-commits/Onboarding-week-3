## H2: What Is a Loyalty Discount? — 250 words

- Definition: a price reduction or reward **earned** by customers through repeated purchases or program participation — not given to everyone

### H3: How Loyalty Discounts Work (Earned vs. Given)
- The core distinction: **earned vs. given**
  - Promotional discount = brand gives up margin proactively to trigger a purchase from anyone
  - Loyalty discount = customer earns it through behavior; brand rewards past action
- Why this matters: earned discounts trigger **reciprocity** (psychological principle), not deal-hunting
- Stat: Acquiring a new customer costs 5–7x more than retaining one (Bain/Invesp) — a small earned discount is a bargain by comparison

*~80 words per subsection. Short intro: different types reinforce different behaviors — choose by what you want to drive.*

- **H3: Points-based redemption discounts** — earn points per purchase, redeem for discount codes. Example: Starbucks Rewards (stars → free items, not blanket % off — keeps ASP intact). → Internal link: Loyalty Points Program Explained
- **H3: Tier-based unlock discounts** — better discount access at higher tiers. Example: Sephora Beauty Insider — discounts unlock only at Rouge tier, protecting margins at entry level. → Internal link: Tiered Loyalty Program Examples
- **H3: Referral discounts** — reward referrer + referred friend with a discount code. Acquisition cost = cost of two discount codes vs. $20–50 paid CAC.
- **H3: Birthday & anniversary discounts** — time-based, personal. High redemption rates; creates emotional connection without triggering deal-hunting behavior.
- **H3: Cashback as discount** — percentage of spend returned as store credit. Keeps money in the ecosystem rather than eroding margin outright. → Internal link: Cashback Loyalty Programs Guide
- **H3: Surprise & delight (flash rewards)** — unannounced discount to VIP members. Drives emotional loyalty because it can't be anticipated or gamed.

## H2: Why Loyalty Discounts Work: The Margin Math — 350 words

- State the fear plainly: "I'm giving away margin on customers who would have paid full price anyway."
- The math that flips it:
  - At 2% reward value: $100 order → $2 earned by the customer
  - If that $2 brings them back for order #2, CAC for order #2 = $2
  - Typical paid CAC: $20–50 — the loyalty discount is 10–25x cheaper
- Stat: Loyalty members generate 12–18% more revenue per household/year than non-members (Bond Brand Loyalty 2024)
- Stat: A 5% retention increase drives 25–95% profit growth (Reichheld/Bain — *The Loyalty Effect*)
- The breakeven formula (make it scannable):
  > *Breakeven repeat rate = reward cost per order ÷ incremental gross margin from repeat purchase*
  - Example: 2% reward value, 40% gross margin → discount pays for itself with just one additional purchase per year

## H2: The 3 Warning Signs Your Loyalty Discount Is Eroding Margins — 250 words
*(The "Loyalty Discount Health Check" — unique to Joy, absent from all competitors)*

- **Sign 1: Redemption rate above 80%** — customers are gaming timing; you're subsidizing purchases that would happen anyway. Fix: raise the earn threshold.
- **Sign 2: AOV drops at redemption** — customers redeem only on small baskets to get the discount. Fix: set a minimum redemption basket size.
- **Sign 3: New member spike, repeat purchase rate flat** — signup discount claimed, customers never return. Fix: move the welcome reward to the second purchase, not the first.
- *Joy location: "Joy's analytics flags all three patterns automatically."*

## H2: How to Set the Right Loyalty Discount Value — 350 words

- Standard benchmark: 1–5% of customer spend
- Below 1%: psychologically invisible — redemption collapses (healthy benchmark: 50–70%, Bond/LoyaltyOne)
- Above 5%: margin risk; only justified with very high CLV (luxury, subscription)
- Decision framework by business type (scannable):
  - High-frequency, low-AOV (coffee, beauty consumables) → 3–5%
  - Low-frequency, high-AOV (furniture, electronics) → 1–2% + experiential rewards
  - Subscription → cashback or store credit, not % discount

**Segment your discounts, not just your value:**
Not all customers should receive the same loyalty discount. Segment by purchase behavior (RFM — recency, frequency, monetary value):
- **High-value repeat customers** → premium tier discounts (exclusive %, early access). They've earned it and churn risk is real.
- **At-risk customers** (bought 2-3x then went silent) → targeted re-engagement reward. Time-limited, higher value than standard to trigger return.
- **New customers** → achievable first-milestone incentive. Not a signup discount — a reward after second purchase to build the repeat habit.
