### **H3.1: The Origin of Starbucks Rewards — Visit-Based to Spend-Based (2016)**
*(~70–80 words)*

**Content to write:**
- Originally "My Starbucks Rewards" — earned 1 star per visit regardless of spend
- April 12, 2016: shifted to spend-based model (2 stars per $1) — rewarding high-value customers over frequent low-spend visitors
- Why the shift: stop "transaction splitting" (customers breaking purchases into multiple visits to game stars), improve throughput, prioritize ROI-positive customers
- Impact: higher-spend customers earned rewards faster; low-spend customers needed 31+ visits vs. previous 12
- Lesson: spend-based earning rewards your best customers, not just your most frequent

**Key claim to make:** The 2016 shift from visits to dollars was Starbucks' first signal that loyalty should reward value, not just frequency.

### **H3.1: Starbucks Loyalty Program Changes — What Went Wrong with the 2026 Rollout**
*(~90–110 words)*

**Content to write:**
- March 10, 2026 launch: members logged in to find their status labeled "Green" — many assumed they'd been demoted
- Root cause: Starbucks discontinued Gold status in 2019 — but the 2026 relaunch reopened that communication channel, making longtime members feel they "lost" a status they hadn't actively held for 7 years
- Earning devaluation: base members earn minimum 25% fewer stars per dollar than pre-2026; only Reserve members come out ahead
- Forrester's John Pedini: "the brand unintentionally sparked a sense of loss before customers ever reached the positive changes"
- Social media firestorm: "I am so done" sentiment across platforms (Newsweek, The Takeout, PYMNTS coverage)

**Key claim to make:** Starbucks introduced tangibly better rewards — and still triggered mass outrage — proving that HOW you communicate loyalty changes matters as much as WHAT you change.

**Needs clarification:** None — Forrester, Inc., Newsweek, PYMNTS all confirmed.

---

### **H3.2: Forrester's Analysis — Communication Failures and the "Perceived Downgrade" Problem**
*(~80–100 words)*

**Content to write:**
- Forrester identified 3 critical communication failures:
  1. **No expectation audit:** Brand didn't assess what members believed their current status was before announcing changes
  2. **Loss-framing:** The announcement led with structural changes (new tiers), not with benefits — members saw what they "lost" before they saw what they gained
  3. **Assumed comprehension:** No education campaign explaining the transition — members were expected to figure it out themselves
- Forrester's guidance: "audit member expectations, prepare transparent explanations, create a communication plan that reinforces value at every step"
- Key research: "well over half" of loyalty members say special treatment is important — perceived status loss triggers disproportionate backlash

**Key claim to make:** Forrester's critique boils down to one rule: never announce what members lose before showing them what they gain.

**Needs clarification:** None — directly from Forrester blog post.

---

### **H3.3: 3 Change Management Rules for Merchants Evolving Their Loyalty Programs**
*(~80–90 words)*

**Content to write:**
- **Rule 1: Audit before announcing.** Survey existing members about what they value most. Know their mental model of their status before you change it.
- **Rule 2: Lead with gains, not structure.** Frame every change as "here's what you're getting" before "here's what's changing." Benefits first, mechanics second.
- **Rule 3: Educate across every touchpoint.** Don't assume one email explains it. Use app notifications, in-store signage, FAQ pages, and social media to reinforce the value story over weeks, not days.
- These rules apply whether you're adding a tier, changing point values, or restructuring rewards — the psychology is the same

**Key claim to make:** The Starbucks backlash is the most expensive loyalty communication failure of 2026 — these three rules ensure your program changes don't trigger the same reaction.

**Needs clarification:** None — framework derived from Forrester analysis.
