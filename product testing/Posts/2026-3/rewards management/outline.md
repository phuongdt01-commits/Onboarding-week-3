## H2: The 5 Components of a Rewards Management System (~700 words)

Core framework. Each H3 follows: **What it is → Why it matters → What good looks like → What bad looks like**

---

### H3: 1. Reward Structure — Which Reward Types to Offer

- The default (percentage discount) is the weakest option: trains customers to wait for deals, erodes margin, zero differentiation from any competitor who can match the same percentage
- **Better options:** product rewards, branded merchandise, early access to new launches, exclusive experiences
- **Vinamilk case study (Joy):** Two redemption paths — discount codes vs. branded physical gifts (stuffed animals, branded merchandise shipped to the door). Gift cost ~$2, perceived value ~$8 — 4x psychological impact vs. an equivalent cash discount. Result: 50% redemption rate. *(Source: Joy Loyalty customer case study)*
- The psychology: gifts create anticipation and identity association; discounts create transactions and price anchoring

**Common reward types in ecommerce loyalty programs:**
  1. **Percentage-off discounts** — easy to understand, but anchors customers to lower prices; typically 5–15% off
  2. **Fixed-amount discounts** — less price-anchoring than percentage; typically $5–$20 off
  3. **Product rewards** — branded merchandise, gift cards, or exclusive products; higher perceived value per dollar spent (Vinamilk case: $2 gift = $8 perceived value)
  4. **Pay-with-points** — customer pays partially or fully with points instead of cash; appeals to points-rich members wanting to avoid checkout friction
  5. **Early/exclusive access** — new product launch access, limited edition drops, VIP sale hours; builds identity and urgency without margin erosion
  6. **Charitable donations** — charity matching in member's name; builds brand loyalty through values alignment (especially Gen Z / millennial segments)
  7. **Experiential rewards** — exclusive events, personalized product consultations, member-only community access

- **What good looks like:** 2–3 redemption options covering both savings motivation (discount/points off) and identity motivation (branded product, early access, or experiential)
- **What bad looks like:** single generic coupon, percentage-off only, no product reward option

---

### H3: 2. Earning Rules — How Customers Accumulate Points (and What That Data Tells You)

- Purchase-only earning caps program relevance to buying cycles. If customers can only earn by purchasing, the program is irrelevant at every other touchpoint in the relationship
- Non-purchase earning extends touchpoints: referrals, approved reviews, birthday registration, social follows, account creation
- **Why earning data matters beyond the points:** a member who earns from a referral is a fundamentally different customer profile than one who earns only on purchases. Earning behavior is a behavioral signal — segment accordingly
- **What good looks like:** 1 point per $1 purchase baseline + 3–5 active non-purchase earning actions
- **What bad looks like:** purchase-only earning with no data surfaced between buying cycles

### H3: 3. Redemption Mechanics — Removing Friction at the Moment That Matters

- High thresholds kill programs. If a customer needs $1,000 in spend to redeem anything meaningful, they do the math once and disengage permanently
- **Threshold design rule:** early win within 2–3 orders. The first redemption moment creates the behavioral loop. If it never happens, the loop never forms.
- **Placement matters as much as timing:** redemption embedded at checkout converts at a higher rate than a separate loyalty portal. Every extra click between "I have points" and "I used them" is a drop-off point
- **Minimum purchase requirement:** protects margin without blocking redemption entirely — set it, but keep it low
- **What good looks like:** meaningful reward accessible within 2–3 orders, checkout-embedded redemption, min purchase requirement set
- **What bad looks like:** 10,000-point threshold, standalone portal, no minimum order protection on margin

---

### H3: 4. Behavioral Triggers — The System That Runs Without You

- The 45–90 day window after a first purchase is when most at-risk customers decide whether to return or disappear
- **Five triggers to activate first, in priority order:**
  1. Win-back at 30 days — bonus points offer to customers who haven't purchased
  2. Milestone nudge — "you're 100 points from a reward" notification
  3. Points expiry warning — 7 days before expiry
  4. Birthday reward — auto-award 7 days before birthday
  5. VIP tier achievement — congratulations message on tier advancement
- **Starbucks reference:** 34.2 million 90-day active Rewards members (Starbucks Q4 FY2025 earnings). That program performance doesn't come from a static points structure — it comes from personalized triggers firing at the right moment for each individual member's behavior pattern

### H3: 5. Analytics — Measuring What Actually Drives Revenue

- **Vanity metrics:** enrollment count, points issued — they don't tell you if the program is changing behavior
- **Revenue metrics to track:** Assisted Orders (purchases where a loyalty touchpoint influenced the buy), repeat purchase rate of members vs. non-members, points liability ratio
- Assisted Orders is what justifies the program to a CFO — map every behavioral trigger back to this number, not to email open rates

## H2: The 3 Failure Modes of Unmanaged Programs (~250 words)

Credibility section. Shows the reader why their program might be underperforming right now.

### H3: 1. Set-and-Forget — The Plateau Pattern
- Program launches, sits untouched. No triggers activated. No reward types tested. No monthly review.
- Result: members sign up, points accumulate, nothing redeems, program flatlines
- What active management looks like in practice: monthly KPI review, one trigger activated per quarter, A/B test on reward structure every 6 months

### H3: 2. Measuring Enrollment Instead of Revenue
- 10,000 members with a 4% redemption rate = cost center
- 2,000 members with a 30%+ redemption rate = growth lever
- The distinction: enrollment numbers show that your program exists. Assisted Orders revenue shows that it works.

### H3: 3. Defaulting to Discounts
- Percentage-off rewards anchor customers on a discounted price and train them to wait for deals
- Branded products, early access, and exclusive experiences build loyalty through identity, not through price conditioning
- Return to Vinamilk: the $2 gift outperforming the $8 discount isn't an anomaly — it's the direct consequence of choosing the right reward type

## H2: How to Set Up Rewards Management on Shopify — 5 Steps (~350 words)

Action framework. Each step maps to one of the 5 components.

### Step 1: Choose Your Reward Structure
- Start with 2 redemption options minimum: points-for-discount + points-for-product
- Set a minimum purchase requirement on all redemptions to protect margin
- If the brand has strong visual identity: add a branded merchandise option as the third tier

### Step 2: Configure Earning Rules
- Baseline: 1 point per $1 purchase
- Add 3–5 non-purchase earning actions — referral (highest CLV impact), approved review, birthday registration, social follow, account creation
- Launch these in order of CLV impact, not ease of setup

### Step 3: Set Redemption Thresholds
- Target: first meaningful redemption achievable within 2–3 orders
- Embed redemption at checkout, not in a separate portal
- Minimum purchase requirement: low enough to not block redemption, high enough to protect margin

## H2: The 4 Metrics That Prove Your Rewards Management Is Working (~250 words)

Monthly audit framework.

1. **Redemption rate** — % of earned points actually redeemed. Target range: 20–35%. Below 10% signals threshold is too high or rewards aren't compelling enough.
2. **Repeat purchase rate: members vs. non-members** — loyalty members should repurchase at a meaningfully higher rate than non-members. If the gap is small, the program isn't changing behavior — it's rewarding behavior that would have happened anyway.
3. **Assisted Orders revenue** — direct revenue attributed to loyalty-influenced purchases. Win-backs that converted, milestone nudges that triggered orders, VIP members who bought at higher AOV. This is what you report to leadership.
4. **Points liability ratio** — unredeemed points outstanding vs. redemption velocity. Growing liability + flat redemption = your reward structure, thresholds, or communication has broken down. Address it before it compounds.

## Final Takeaways (~150 words)

5 bullets — one per component + the core distinction.

- **Rewards management ≠ having a loyalty program.** The program is the structure. Management is what makes it compound.
- **Reward structure determines redemption rate.** The Vinamilk case study proves it: a $2 gift outperforms an $8 discount in perceived value. Default to product rewards, not percentage discounts.
- **Earning rules are also data collection.** What a member earns points for tells you who they are. Use it.
- **Behavioral triggers are the engine.** Starbucks doesn't have 34.2 million active members because of its points structure. It has them because the right offer fires at the right moment.
- **Assisted Orders is the only metric that justifies the program to leadership.** Track it from day one.

Closing conviction line: the merchants who dominate in retention aren't running more sophisticated programs. They're running them more deliberately.
