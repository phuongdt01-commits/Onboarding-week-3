# Joy Loyalty Brand Voice

## Core Mission
Help Shopify merchants build lasting customer relationships that drive sustainable business growth through meaningful loyalty experiences.

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Knowledgeable
Express experience through clear, generous sharing - not by claiming expertise.

**What this means:**
- Show understanding through practical examples and use cases
- Reference Shopify-native concepts naturally (Flow, metafields, tags, app blocks, POS)
- Explain the "why" behind features, not just the "how"
- Share what works for real merchants

**What this is NOT:**
- Saying "we're experts" or "industry-leading"
- Using internal jargon or technical terms merchants don't understand
- Explaining without context or real-world application

### 2. Supportive
Speak like a thoughtful teammate: empathetic, encouraging, never pushy.

**What this means:**
- Guide without pressure
- Acknowledge merchant challenges and validate their concerns
- Offer help that fits their specific situation
- Stay calm and reassuring, especially in support contexts

**What this is NOT:**
- Being overly apologetic ("sorry" as filler)
- Pushing features or upselling
- Dismissing concerns or passing problems along
- Using "unfortunately" without offering alternatives

### 3. Humble
Don't exaggerate or overpromise. If we can't prove it, we don't claim it.

**What this means:**
- Let usefulness speak for itself
- Acknowledge limitations honestly
- Say "currently not supported, but here's a workaround"
- Use phrases like "some merchants choose to..." instead of prescribing

**What this is NOT:**
- Superlatives: "most powerful", "ultimate", "game-changing"
- Overpromising: "perfect solution", "everything you need"
- Vague claims without evidence
- Avoiding difficult topics

### 4. Clear
Write with structure, use familiar terms, guide without fluff.

**What this means:**
- Short, natural phrases that match merchant thinking
- Shopify-native terminology (not invented names)
- Action-oriented verbs: Create, Set up, View, Change
- Concrete examples over abstract concepts

**What this is NOT:**
- Technical jargon: "earning transformation ratio", "dynamic UI extension"
- Vague labels: "Add logic", "Enable flexibility"
- Weak verbs: "Go", "Click here", "Manage"
- Internal system behavior merchants don't control

### 5. Inspirational
Offer optional best practices, ideas, and use cases that help merchants go further.

**What this means:**
- Show what's possible beyond basic setup
- Include soft suggestions: "You might try...", "Some merchants..."
- Share tactical workflows that inspire experimentation
- End with optional depth or tips

**What this is NOT:**
- Stopping at basic configuration
- Prescribing "best practices" as the only way
- Making hypothetical assumptions
- Over-explaining internal tech

## Voice Adaptation by Context

### Product UI
**Tone:** Concise, quietly helpful
**Focus:** Intuitive actions, useful tooltips, no fluff
**Example:** "Best for stores focused on repeat purchases. Higher tiers unlock as customers spend more."

### Help Center
**Tone:** Calm, instructional, structured
**Focus:** Goal-oriented solutions, plain language, anticipate questions
**Example:** "Go to Program Settings > Rewards. Click 'Create reward' and choose your type."

### Video Tutorials
**Tone:** Calm confidence with geeky enthusiasm
**Focus:** Feature clarity, best practices, what else is possible
**Example:** "You can even combine it with metafields and cart conditions if you want to get fancy."

### Support
**Tone:** Supportive, humble, clear
**Focus:** Build trust, solve problems, leave merchants capable
**Example:** "I see what's happening - let's walk through this together and get it sorted."

### Emails
**Tone:** Gentle, informative, encouraging discovery
**Focus:** Show value, suggest next steps, no pressure
**Example:** "Your loyalty program is live. Next, you might want to explore setting up a referral reward."

### Landing Pages
**Tone:** Honest, calm, value-focused
**Focus:** Present benefits clearly, no hype
**Example:** "Flexible rewards, Shopify-native integration, and deep customization - without extra complexity."

### Sales
**Tone:** Calm expertise, advisory
**Focus:** Listen, understand needs, offer right guidance
**Example:** "There's no perfect one-size-fits-all loyalty setup - but here's what we've seen work for brands in your position."

### Crisis
**Tone:** Calm accountability
**Focus:** Acknowledge clearly, explain what's happening, realistic timeline
**Example:** "We're seeing reports of points not updating correctly. Our team is investigating and we'll have an update within 2 hours."

## Shopify-Native Terminology

Always use these Shopify terms:
- **Flow** (not automation engine)
- **Metafields** (not custom fields)
- **Tags** (not labels)
- **App blocks** (not extensions)
- **Customers** (not users)
- **Orders** (not transactions)
- **Points** and **Rewards** (clear, simple)
- **POS** (point of sale)
- **Customer Accounts** (not user profiles)

## Label vs Tooltip Pattern

Use this 3-part structure:
1. What it's for - Start with real use case
2. What it does - Be concrete without technical jargon
3. (Optional) How to use it well - Add a nudge or idea

Example:
- **Label:** "Points expire after 180 days"
- **Tooltip:** "Encourages customers to come back before losing their balance. You can adjust this under Program Settings."

## Do's and Don'ts

### ✅ Do
- Use Shopify-native terms
- Write like a person guiding another person
- Start with clarity, end with optional depth
- Include soft suggestions: "You might try..."
- Say "Currently not supported - but here's a workaround"
- Reflect real merchant behavior and use cases
- Guide with action verbs: Create, Set up, View, Change
- Focus on what merchants control and why it matters
- Use examples merchants can relate to
- Let usefulness speak for the product

### ❌ Don't
- Invent new names for Shopify features
- Sound robotic or overly formal
- Stop at basic steps without context
- Prescribe fixed setups without nuance
- Avoid topics or overpromise
- Make hypothetical assumptions
- Use weak labels: "Go", "Click here", "Manage logic"
- Overemphasize internal tech merchants don't care about
- Use technical jargon without explanation
- Rely on superlatives: "most powerful", "game-changing", "ultimate"

## Crisis Communication Principles

When things go wrong:
1. **Acknowledge clearly** - Don't be vague or defensive
2. **Explain what's happening** - Be transparent about the issue
3. **Give realistic timeline** - Don't say "soon", give actual hours/minutes
4. **Offer immediate help** - Provide action steps or manual assistance
5. **Take ownership** - Don't pass the buck or blame
6. **Follow up** - Commit to updates and keep that commitment

Avoid:
- "We're sorry for any inconvenience" (vague apology)
- "Technical difficulties" (jargon)
- "We'll get back to you soon" (no timeline)
- "I'll escalate this" (without action)
- Over-apologizing as filler

## Final Principle

**Deliver more than expected** - not just in features, but in every word, every answer, every piece of guidance.

If we name it Joy Loyalty Program, it has to be a joy to use.
