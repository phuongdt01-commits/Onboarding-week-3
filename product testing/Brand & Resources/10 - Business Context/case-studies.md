# Loyalty Program Case Studies

> Real-world examples of loyalty programs in action. Use these to illustrate business impact in content.

---

## Vinamilk — FMCG Loyalty at Scale

### About Vinamilk
Vinamilk is one of Vietnam's largest FMCG brands (dairy products, $500M+ annual revenue). They have traditionally distributed through a wide retail and distributor network, but have been transitioning to a D2C model — building direct relationships with end consumers.

### The Program
Vinamilk built a comprehensive loyalty program as part of their shift to D2C.

**Key results:**
- **Redemption rate: 50%** — exceptionally high for FMCG (industry average is much lower)
- Total marketing/promotion budget: **10,400 billion VND (~$430M)** in 2024
- Shifted ~300 billion VND from traditional advertising → promotional/loyalty spend

### How It Works (Two Pillars)
1. **Discount codes:** Redeem points for discounts on next purchase — drives repeat purchases
2. **Physical gifts:** Redeem points for branded physical goods (stuffed animals, backpacks, branded items) — delivered to home or collected at retail

### Why Gifts vs. Discounts?
A gift costing 50,000 VND (~$2) to source can be perceived as having 200,000 VND (~$8) in value — **4x the psychological impact** of an equivalent cash discount.

For Vinamilk specifically, direct discounts could destabilize their distributor pricing structure. Gifts sidestep that problem entirely while delivering greater perceived value.

### Key Takeaway for Content
Loyalty programs work beyond e-commerce — in FMCG, physical gifts create stronger emotional connections than cash-equivalent discounts. The "value" customers perceive matters more than the actual cost to the brand.

## AliExpress / Temu — Referral at Massive Scale

### AliExpress (Passive Model)
AliExpress kept product prices extremely low by not spending on direct-to-consumer advertising. Instead, they relied on distributors and resellers to bring products to market. Result: product reached consumers, but brand was rarely top-of-mind for Western buyers.

### Temu (Active Referral Model)
When Temu entered the US market in 2024, they couldn't rely on the same passive approach because:
- Consumer expectations for brand recognition were higher
- Competition required direct consumer engagement

**Solution:** Combined two strategies:
1. Large traditional ad spend to build brand awareness quickly
2. Referral/group-buying model (borrowed from Pinduoduo) to create social viral growth

**Result:** Fast user acquisition + low per-unit cost = rapid market penetration while keeping prices competitive.

### Key Takeaway for Content
Referrals aren't just a feature — they're a distribution strategy. For merchants spending heavily on acquisition, a well-designed referral program can replicate word-of-mouth growth at scale with a fraction of the ad cost.

## Writing With Case Studies

When using case studies in Joy content:

1. **Lead with the business problem**, not the feature used
2. **Show the metric** — redemption rate, revenue lift, repeat purchase rate
3. **Connect to the reader's situation** — "If Vinamilk with 500M in revenue uses loyalty to reduce ad dependency, what does that mean for a $100K/month Shopify brand?"
4. **Use Joy's vocabulary** — "Vinamilk **crushed** their redemption targets..." / "Temu **dominated** user acquisition with referral mechanics..."
