# What Is Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT)?

**Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT)** is a CX metric that measures how satisfied a customer feels immediately after a specific interaction - a purchase, a support ticket, a delivery. It's captured through a single survey question, typically rated on a 1-5 or 1-10 scale.

CSAT is transactional by nature. It tells you how someone felt at a moment in time, not whether they'll come back. That's a meaningful limitation - and it's what makes CSAT different from longer-term metrics like Net Promoter Score or Customer Lifetime Value. Think of it as a snapshot, not a trajectory. Used well, those snapshots reveal exactly which parts of the customer experience are working and which aren't.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** (Satisfied responses / Total responses) x 100 = CSAT %; satisfied responses are typically defined as 4s and 5s on a 5-point scale.
- **Benchmark:** Most e-commerce brands target 75-85% CSAT; scores above 85% are considered excellent and scores below 60% indicate a specific friction point worth diagnosing.

## FAQs

### How is CSAT calculated?
CSAT is calculated by dividing the number of satisfied responses (typically 4s and 5s on a 5-point scale) by the total number of responses, then multiplying by 100. The formula is: (Satisfied responses / Total responses) x 100 = CSAT %. A score of 80% means 80 out of every 100 respondents rated the interaction positively. Simple math, but the insight it produces depends entirely on when and where you ask the question.

### What's the difference between CSAT and NPS?
CSAT measures satisfaction with a specific interaction - it's narrow and immediate. NPS (Net Promoter Score) measures overall brand sentiment and the likelihood to recommend. Think of CSAT as a pulse check on a single touchpoint and NPS as a read on the entire relationship. They're not competing metrics. E-commerce brands often use both: CSAT post-purchase to catch friction fast, NPS to track whether loyalty is actually building over time.

### What is a good CSAT score?
Benchmarks vary by industry, but most e-commerce brands aim for a CSAT of 75-85%. Scores above 85% are considered excellent. Scores below 60% are a signal that something specific in the customer journey - fulfillment, support response time, returns - is creating friction worth fixing. That's the number to watch: not just the average, but whether a specific touchpoint is consistently dragging scores down. The American Customer Satisfaction Index (ACSI) publishes sector-specific benchmarks annually if you want an external reference point.

### How do e-commerce brands use CSAT?
Brands use CSAT surveys at high-stakes moments: post-delivery, post-support interaction, and post-returns. The data flags individual pain points before they become churn drivers. And when paired with a loyalty program, CSAT scores can identify which reward experiences are landing well and which aren't. A product quiz that delights customers will show up differently in the data than a redemption flow that frustrates them. That's the kind of signal most brands don't have without deliberately measuring for it.

## Related Terms

- **Net Promoter Score (NPS).** A brand loyalty metric that asks customers how likely they are to recommend the company - broader in scope than CSAT.
- **Customer Effort Score (CES).** Measures how easy it was for a customer to complete an interaction, often used alongside CSAT for a fuller picture.
- **Customer churn rate.** The percentage of customers who don't return. High CSAT scores at key touchpoints are associated with lower churn.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The total revenue expected from a customer over time. Sustained CSAT improvements feed into higher CLV over time.
