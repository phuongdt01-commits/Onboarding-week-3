# What Is Product Margin?

**Product margin** is the percentage of a product's revenue that remains as profit after subtracting the direct cost to produce or acquire it. It's the per-unit measure of how much a sale actually contributes to the business before operating expenses are considered. Higher product margin means more room to fund marketing, operations, and growth.

The formula is:

> Formula: Product Margin (%) = (Selling Price - COGS) / Selling Price x 100

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** Product Margin (%) = (Selling Price - COGS) / Selling Price x 100.
- **Benchmark:** Margins vary significantly by category: consumer electronics and commodities typically run 10-30%, apparel 40-60%, and beauty/personal care 60-80%.
- **Rule of thumb:** Product margin must be high enough to cover CAC, fulfillment, and overhead and still leave net profit - the absolute percentage is less important than that test.

## FAQs

### How is product margin calculated?
Product margin is calculated by subtracting the cost of goods sold (COGS) from the selling price, then dividing that result by the selling price and multiplying by 100 to express it as a percentage. A product that sells for $50 with a COGS of $20 has a product margin of 60%. This is also called gross margin at the product level - the terms are often used interchangeably. Knowing your number here is non-negotiable before you set pricing or plan any reward spend.

### What's the difference between product margin and markup?
Both measure the relationship between cost and selling price, but from different angles. Margin is calculated as a percentage of the selling price. Markup is calculated as a percentage of the cost. A product that costs $20 and sells for $50 has a 60% margin but a 150% markup. They're not the same number - and mixing them up causes real pricing mistakes. Margin is the more commonly used metric in retail and e-commerce financial reporting.

### What is a good product margin for e-commerce?
It depends heavily on the category. Consumer electronics and commodity goods typically operate at 10-30% margins. Apparel ranges from 40-60%. Beauty and personal care - think brands like Sephora's own lines - often run 60-80%. Software and digital products can exceed 80%. The key benchmark isn't the absolute margin percentage. It's whether product margin is high enough to cover customer acquisition costs, fulfillment, and overhead while leaving net profit. That's what actually matters.

### How does product margin affect retention strategy?
Margin sets the budget for everything that happens after the first sale. A high product margin gives brands the room to offer meaningful loyalty rewards, run promotions, and invest in retention without destroying profitability. Brands with thin margins have to be more selective - the economics of repeat purchases need to work harder to justify any reward spend. You can't build a generous loyalty program on a 15% margin without getting the math right first.

## Related Terms

- **Gross margin.** The aggregate version of product margin across an entire store or product line, expressed as a percentage of total revenue.
- **Cost of goods sold (COGS).** The direct costs that are subtracted from selling price to calculate product margin.
- **Net profit margin.** Margin calculated after all expenses - operating costs, taxes, and interest - are deducted, not just COGS.
- **Average order value (AOV).** The mean transaction size that, combined with product margin, determines the revenue contribution of each order.
