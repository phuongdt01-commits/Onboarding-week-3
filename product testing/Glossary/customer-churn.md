# What Is Customer Churn?

**Customer churn** is the percentage of customers who stop purchasing from a brand within a given time period. It's the direct inverse of retention rate, and it's one of the most consequential metrics for any e-commerce business trying to build sustainable revenue.

Churn falls into two categories: voluntary churn, where customers actively decide to leave, and involuntary churn, where they're lost due to payment failures or billing errors. Both eat into CLV and drive up the effective cost of growth. The distinction matters because each type has a different fix.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** Churn rate = Churned customers / Total customers at start of period.
- **Red flag threshold:** Monthly churn above 5% means average customer lifespan is 20 months - usually too short for standard CLV models to deliver margin.
- **Benchmark:** Most DTC brands target annual churn below 20-30%. A 5% increase in retention rates leads to a 25-95% increase in profits ([Reichheld & Sasser, HBR 1990](https://hbr.org/1990/09/zero-defections-quality-comes-to-services)).

## FAQs

### What is customer churn in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, churn is typically measured over a 30, 60, or 90-day window, depending on the brand's expected purchase frequency. A customer who bought from a furniture brand 18 months ago isn't churned - that's a normal repurchase cycle. A customer who bought a daily supplement 45 days ago and hasn't reordered probably is. Context and category matter when defining what "churned" actually means for your business. Get that definition wrong and you'll be optimizing for the wrong number.

### What's the difference between customer churn rate and revenue churn rate?
Customer churn rate measures the percentage of customers lost. Revenue churn rate measures the percentage of revenue lost. They don't move in lockstep. If your highest-value customers churn at higher rates than average, revenue churn will be worse than customer churn suggests. If low-spend customers churn more, revenue churn will look better. Both numbers deserve attention. Customer churn rate alone can mask serious revenue exposure - and it often does.

### What is a good customer churn rate for e-commerce?
E-commerce churn rates vary significantly by category, but most DTC brands target an annual churn rate below 20-30%. Monthly churn above 5% is generally a red flag - at 5% monthly, average customer lifespan is 20 months, which is usually too short for the CLV math to work unless your gross margins are unusually high. Subscription-specific businesses often see monthly involuntary churn rates of 1-3% on top of voluntary losses. Research by Frederick Reichheld and W. Earl Sasser, [published in Harvard Business Review (1990)](https://hbr.org/1990/09/zero-defections-quality-comes-to-services), found that a 5% increase in customer retention rates leads to a 25-95% increase in profits - the range is wide because it's category-dependent, but the direction is consistent. Brands with strong retention programs - Chewy and Dollar Shave Club are cited examples - consistently operate with churn rates well below category averages. The benchmark is directional; your category sets the floor.

### How do you calculate customer churn rate?
Churn rate = churned customers / total customers at the start of the period. If you started a month with 1,000 active customers and 80 didn't return, your churn rate is 8%. The harder question is defining "active" - brands often use RFM (recency, frequency, monetary) scoring to classify who counts as a current customer versus lapsed. The formula is simple. The definition work is where most merchants get stuck.

## Related Terms

- **Retention rate.** The inverse of churn rate - the percentage of customers who return. Churn rate + retention rate = 100%.
- **Involuntary churn.** Churn caused by payment failures rather than a customer's decision to leave.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The total revenue expected from a single customer. High churn compresses CLV directly.
- **Repeat purchase rate.** The percentage of customers who buy more than once. A leading indicator of whether churn is likely to be a problem.
