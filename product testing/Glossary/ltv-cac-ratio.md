# What Is the LTV:CAC Ratio?

**The LTV:CAC ratio** is the comparison of customer lifetime value (LTV) to customer acquisition cost (CAC), expressed as a ratio. It answers a single foundational question: is the revenue generated from a customer worth more than what it cost to acquire them - and by how much?

LTV:CAC = lifetime value / customer acquisition cost. It's one of the most watched metrics in e-commerce and DTC because it captures the efficiency of a brand's entire growth model in a single number.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** LTV:CAC = Customer Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost.
- **Benchmark:** A ratio of 3:1 is the widely cited minimum for a viable model; most healthy DTC brands operate between 3:1 and 5:1.
- **Rule of thumb:** A ratio above 5:1 can signal underinvestment in acquisition; below 1:1 is a structural problem that retention alone cannot fix.

## FAQs

### What is LTV:CAC in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, LTV:CAC is the core test of whether a paid acquisition strategy is economically sustainable. If a brand's CAC is $80 and its LTV is $240, the ratio is 3:1 - the common baseline for a viable model. But if CAC climbs because of rising ad costs while LTV stays flat, that ratio deteriorates fast. And when it does, the growth model starts to break. Brands with strong retention programs and high repeat purchase rates can sustain higher CAC because their LTV is higher - giving them a structural advantage in acquisition markets that brands without retention programs simply don't have.

### What's the difference between LTV:CAC ratio and CAC payback period?
They measure related but distinct things. LTV:CAC compares total lifetime revenue to acquisition cost - it tells you the overall return on a customer. CAC payback period measures how many months it takes to recover CAC from gross margin - that's the cash efficiency question. A brand can have a strong LTV:CAC ratio but a long payback period if most of the lifetime value arrives late in the customer relationship. Both metrics together give a full picture of customer economics. Neither one alone is sufficient.

### What is a good LTV:CAC ratio for e-commerce?
The widely cited benchmark is 3:1 or higher. A ratio of 3:1 means you generate $3 in lifetime revenue for every $1 spent acquiring a customer. Below 1:1 means you're spending more to acquire customers than they're worth - that's a structural problem, not a marketing problem. Above 5:1 can signal underinvestment in acquisition: the brand may be leaving growth on the table by not deploying capital into channels that work. Most healthy DTC brands operate in the 3:1 to 5:1 range. That's the number to watch.

### How do you improve the LTV:CAC ratio?
It improves when LTV goes up, CAC comes down, or both move at once. LTV increases through higher purchase frequency, increased average order value, and lower churn. CAC decreases through better conversion rate optimization, stronger creative performance, or shifting spend toward owned and earned channels - email, referral, loyalty. Brands that invest in post-purchase retention improve the ratio from the LTV side without touching acquisition budgets. That's often the faster path, because you're working with customers you already have.

## Related Terms

- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The numerator in the LTV:CAC ratio. The total expected revenue from a single customer relationship.
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** The denominator. The total cost of acquiring a new customer, including all marketing and sales expenses.
- **CAC payback period.** The number of months needed to recover CAC from gross margin. A companion metric to LTV:CAC that captures timing and cash efficiency.
- **Repeat purchase rate.** One of the key drivers of LTV. Higher repeat purchase rate extends customer lifespan and grows LTV, improving the ratio from the top.
