# What Is a Loyalty Point?

A **loyalty point** is a unit of virtual currency that a customer earns through qualifying purchases or actions in a loyalty program and later redeems for rewards, discounts, or perks. Points are the core mechanic of most loyalty programs - they're what gives customers a tangible, trackable reason to return.

Every points program sets two variables: the earn rate (how many points per dollar spent) and the redemption value (what a point is worth at checkout). At Starbucks, Stars earned on purchases redeem for free drinks. At Marriott Bonvoy, points earned on hotel stays redeem for free nights or flights. The ratio between those two numbers determines the program's actual generosity. Everything else is packaging.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** Effective return rate = (redemption value earned / dollars spent) x 100; a customer spending $100 who earns $2 in redeemable value has an effective return rate of 2%.
- **Decision rule:** Set earn rate high enough to feel motivating within a few purchases, but low enough that redemption liability doesn't compress margins.

## FAQs

### What is a loyalty point worth?
It depends entirely on how the program is structured. A common setup is 1 point per $1 spent, with 100 points redeeming for $1 off - making each point worth $0.01. Some programs set higher earn rates with lower redemption values to make balances grow fast while limiting liability. The clearest way to evaluate a program's generosity is to calculate the effective return rate: if a customer spends $100 and earns enough points to redeem $2 in value, the effective return is 2%. That's the number that tells you whether the program is actually competitive.

### What's the difference between a loyalty point and a loyalty credit or cashback?
Points and cashback both reward spend, but they work differently in the customer's psychology. A point balance is a number that grows - it creates a sense of accumulation and progress toward a redemption moment. Cashback is more immediate and transparent: a percentage of spend returned as currency. Points programs tend to generate higher engagement because the earn-and-redeem cycle creates behavioral loops that pure cashback doesn't. But cashback wins on clarity. The tradeoff is real.

### How do brands calculate the right earn rate for loyalty points?
Earn rate decisions come down to two things: competitive positioning and margin. A brand needs to set an earn rate high enough to feel motivating - customers won't engage with a program where it takes 50 purchases to earn anything meaningful - but not so high that redemption liability compresses margins. Most brands benchmark their earn rate against competitors in the same category before setting their own. And they should. A program that's less generous than a direct competitor is easy to walk away from.

### What happens to unredeemed loyalty points?
Unredeemed points sit on a brand's balance sheet as loyalty liability - a real financial obligation. Breakage is the industry term for points that are earned but never redeemed, typically due to expiration or account inactivity. High breakage can look good for margins in the short term but it's a strong signal that customers don't value the program. Low redemption rate is one of the clearest early signs that a loyalty program is failing to drive engagement. If customers aren't redeeming, they're not engaged. It's that simple.

## Related Terms

- **Points-based loyalty program.** The loyalty structure that loyalty points power - customers earn and redeem points as the primary reward mechanism.
- **Redemption rate.** The percentage of issued points that customers actually redeem - the primary health metric for any points program.
- **Earn rate.** The number of points awarded per dollar spent - one of the two variables that determine a program's effective generosity.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The revenue metric that points programs aim to grow by creating a structured incentive for repeat purchases.
