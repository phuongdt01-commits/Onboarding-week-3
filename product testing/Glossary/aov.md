# What Is AOV (Average Order Value)?

**Average order value (AOV)** is the mean dollar amount a customer spends each time they place an order in your store. It's one of the three core inputs to customer lifetime value and a direct indicator of how much revenue each transaction generates before costs are considered.

The formula is:

> Formula: AOV = Total Revenue / Number of Orders

AOV is typically calculated over a defined period - monthly, quarterly, or annually.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** AOV = Total Revenue / Number of Orders
- **Formula:** CLTV = AOV x Purchase Frequency x Customer Lifespan, so a 20% AOV increase raises CLTV by 20% all else equal.
- **Benchmark:** Shopify data puts average e-commerce AOV in the $50-$100 range, but the more useful benchmark is margin-adjusted AOV tracked against your own trend.

## FAQs

### How is AOV calculated in e-commerce?
Divide total revenue by the number of orders placed in a given timeframe. A store that generates $150,000 from 3,000 orders in a month has an AOV of $50. Most e-commerce platforms, including Shopify, calculate and display AOV automatically in their analytics dashboards. But it's worth distinguishing AOV from average revenue per user - AOV measures per transaction, not per customer. They sound similar and answer very different questions.

### What's the difference between AOV and revenue per customer?
AOV measures the value of each transaction. Revenue per customer measures the total value across all of a customer's transactions. A customer who places three $50 orders has an AOV of $50 per order but contributes $150 in total revenue. The two metrics aren't interchangeable: AOV is about transaction efficiency, revenue per customer is about relationship depth. You need both to get a full picture.

### What is a good AOV for e-commerce?
AOV benchmarks vary significantly by product type and price point. Shopify data puts the average across e-commerce stores somewhere in the $50-$100 range, but that figure is heavily category-dependent. Luxury and home goods brands often see AOV above $200. The more useful benchmark is tracking your own AOV over time and measuring it against product margin - because a rising AOV on low-margin products doesn't improve profitability the same way it does on high-margin ones. The AOV number alone doesn't tell you much. Margin-adjusted AOV tells you something.

### Why does AOV matter for the CLTV calculation?
AOV is a direct multiplier in CLTV. Since CLTV = AOV x Purchase Frequency x Customer Lifespan, increasing AOV by 20% raises CLTV by 20% - all else equal. That means strategies that lift AOV (bundles, upsells, free shipping thresholds) have a compounding effect on the long-run value of each customer relationship, not just on the individual transaction. Starbucks figured this out a long time ago. A small per-visit spend increase compounds dramatically across thousands of visits per customer.

## Related Terms

- **Customer lifetime value (CLTV).** The total revenue expected from a customer - a metric that AOV feeds directly into via the CLTV formula.
- **Purchase frequency.** How often a customer buys, which multiplies with AOV to determine total customer revenue.
- **Gross merchandise value (GMV).** The aggregate total of all orders, which equals AOV multiplied by order count.
- **Product margin.** The profitability lens that determines whether a higher AOV actually translates to higher profit.
