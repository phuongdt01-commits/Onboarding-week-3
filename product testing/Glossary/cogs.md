# What Is COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)?

**Cost of goods sold (COGS)** is the direct cost of producing or acquiring the products that a business sells in a given period. It includes only the costs tied directly to the goods themselves - not operating expenses like rent, salaries, or marketing. COGS is subtracted from revenue to calculate gross profit.

The formula is:

> Formula: COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory

For e-commerce brands that don't manufacture their own products, COGS is typically the wholesale cost of inventory plus inbound freight.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory; Gross Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue.
- **Rule of thumb:** COGS includes only costs directly tied to goods (inventory, inbound freight, packaging, manufacturing) - outbound shipping, marketing, and salaries are operating expenses and must not be mixed in.
- **Decision rule:** Whichever fulfillment cost treatment is chosen (COGS vs. operating expense) must be applied consistently so that margin comparisons over time remain valid.

## FAQs

### What is included in COGS for e-commerce?
For most e-commerce businesses, COGS includes the cost of purchased inventory, inbound shipping and freight costs, packaging materials that are part of the product, and any manufacturing or assembly costs if the merchant produces their own goods. It doesn't include outbound shipping to customers, advertising spend, platform fees, or staff salaries - those are operating expenses. The line between COGS and operating expenses matters because they appear in different places on the income statement. Mixing them distorts your gross margin, which is a bigger problem than it sounds.

### What's the difference between COGS and operating expenses?
COGS covers costs directly tied to producing or acquiring the goods sold. Operating expenses (OpEx) cover everything else needed to run the business - rent, software, marketing, payroll. Both reduce profitability, but they're reported separately. Gross profit is revenue minus COGS. Operating income is gross profit minus operating expenses. A business can have strong gross profit but poor operating income if overhead is high. That's a useful distinction: it tells you where the problem actually is.

### How does COGS affect gross margin?
Gross margin is calculated as revenue minus COGS, divided by revenue. Lower COGS relative to revenue means higher gross margin - which gives a business more room to spend on marketing, operations, and profit. For Shopify merchants, tracking COGS accurately is essential for understanding true margin per product, not just the selling price. A product priced at $60 with $40 in COGS has a 33% gross margin. Whether that's sufficient depends entirely on what the overhead looks like.

### Does fulfillment cost count as COGS?
It depends on the accounting policy, and opinions genuinely differ. Some e-commerce operators include outbound fulfillment costs - pick, pack, shipping to customer - in COGS to get a fully-loaded unit economics view. Others treat fulfillment as an operating expense. Neither approach is wrong. The more important thing is consistency: whichever method is chosen should be applied uniformly so that margin comparisons over time are actually valid.

## Related Terms

- **Gross profit.** Revenue minus COGS - the first profitability line on the income statement and the direct output of COGS tracking.
- **Product margin.** The per-unit margin calculated using COGS as the cost input: (Selling Price - COGS) / Selling Price.
- **Gross merchandise value (GMV).** The revenue figure that COGS is compared against to determine gross margin.
- **Net profit margin.** The bottom-line profitability metric that accounts for COGS plus all operating expenses.
