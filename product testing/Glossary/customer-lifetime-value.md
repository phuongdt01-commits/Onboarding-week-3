# What Is Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)?

**Customer lifetime value (CLV)** is the total revenue a brand expects to generate from a single customer over the entire length of their relationship. It's the north-star retention metric for e-commerce - higher CLV means each customer is worth more, which means the brand can afford to acquire and retain customers more aggressively.

CLV is sometimes written as CLTV or LTV. All three refer to the same concept. It's used both as a backward-looking measure (what has this cohort actually spent) and a forward-looking prediction (what will a new customer be worth). Most brands track both. The historical version tells you where you've been. The predictive version tells you how much to spend today.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** CLV = Average Order Value x Purchase Frequency x Customer Lifespan. Multiply by gross margin % for a margin-adjusted version. Example: $60 AOV x 5 orders/year x 3 years x 40% margin = $360 CLV.
- **Benchmark:** 3:1 CLV:CAC is the standard minimum target. Top DTC brands in subscription and consumable categories regularly run 5:1 or higher.
- **Decision rule:** CLV sets the ceiling on what you can spend to acquire or retain a customer. Spend above it, and you're buying customers at a loss.

## FAQs

### What is CLV in marketing?
In marketing, CLV is used to determine how much to spend acquiring different customer segments and which retention tactics are worth investing in. If your CLV is $200 and your CAC is $180, you're barely profitable - and any churn before the second purchase destroys the margin entirely. If your CLV is $800 and your CAC is $150, you have room to invest heavily in loyalty, post-purchase experience, and win-back campaigns. CLV is what turns retention from a vague priority into a financially grounded decision. Without it, you're guessing.

### What's the difference between CLV and LTV?
CLV and LTV are the same metric. CLV (customer lifetime value) is the term most commonly used in DTC e-commerce, CRM, and loyalty contexts. LTV (lifetime value) tends to appear in SaaS, VC, and financial reporting. Some teams use LTV to describe historical cohort revenue and CLV for predictive modeling - but that split isn't standard. In most e-commerce contexts, they're interchangeable. Don't let the terminology debate distract from actually calculating the number.

### What is a good CLV for e-commerce?
There's no universal "good" number - CLV is only meaningful relative to CAC. The standard target is a CLV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better - a benchmark widely used in DTC and VC analysis - though top brands in high-frequency categories like subscriptions and consumables regularly run 5:1 or higher. Brands with high purchase frequency and consumable products - pet food, skincare, supplements - tend to have naturally higher CLV than those in low-repeat categories. Chewy, with its Autoship program, systematically increases CLV by locking in purchase frequency at the point of first subscription. That's not an accident. It's a deliberate structural choice.

### How do you calculate customer lifetime value?
The standard formula is: CLV = average order value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan. A margin-adjusted version is more useful for decision-making: CLV = (average order value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan) x gross margin %. For example, a customer who spends $60 per order, orders 5 times per year, stays active for 3 years, and generates 40% gross margin has a CLV of $360. That's the ceiling on what it makes economic sense to spend acquiring and retaining them. Spend more than that, and you're buying customers at a loss.

## Related Terms

- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** The cost of acquiring a new customer. CLV and CAC are the two sides of customer economics - neither is meaningful without the other.
- **LTV:CAC ratio.** The ratio comparing lifetime value to acquisition cost. The standard test of whether a customer acquisition strategy is financially sustainable.
- **Repeat purchase rate.** How often customers come back. Higher repeat purchase rate directly increases CLV by extending customer lifespan and purchase frequency.
- **Churn rate.** The rate at which customers stop buying. Churn cuts CLV short - a 20% annual churn means the average customer lifespan is 5 years, not indefinite.
