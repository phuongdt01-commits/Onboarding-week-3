# What Is ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)?

**Return on ad spend (ROAS)** is a marketing metric that measures how much revenue a business generates for every dollar spent on advertising. It's the standard efficiency measure for paid campaigns across channels like Meta, Google, and TikTok. A ROAS of 4x means $4 in revenue was generated for every $1 spent on ads.

The formula is:

> Formula: ROAS = Revenue from Ads / Ad Spend

ROAS is expressed as a ratio or multiplier, not a percentage.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** ROAS = Revenue from Ads / Ad Spend
- **Formula:** Breakeven ROAS = 1 / Gross Margin Percentage (a brand with 25% gross margin needs at least 4x ROAS to cover product costs on new customer acquisition).
- **Benchmark:** 4x is a common rule of thumb, but the correct target depends entirely on gross margin.

## FAQs

### How is ROAS calculated?
ROAS is calculated by dividing the revenue attributed to an ad campaign by the total spend on that campaign. If you spent $5,000 on ads and those ads drove $20,000 in revenue, your ROAS is 4x (or 4:1). Most ad platforms calculate ROAS automatically within their dashboards - but attribution windows and methods vary. Which is why the same campaign can show different ROAS numbers on Meta and Google simultaneously. Neither is lying. They're just measuring different windows.

### What's the difference between ROAS and ROI?
ROAS measures revenue relative to ad spend only. ROI measures profit relative to total investment - including product costs, overhead, and all other expenses. A campaign with a 4x ROAS can still be unprofitable if the product margin is low and other costs are high. ROAS is a channel efficiency metric. ROI is a profitability metric. Neither replaces the other, and brands that optimize for ROAS without watching margin often find out the hard way.

### What is a good ROAS for e-commerce?
A common rule of thumb is 4x - $4 in revenue for every $1 in ad spend. But the right target ROAS depends entirely on your product margin. A brand with a 70% gross margin can be profitable at 2x ROAS. A brand with a 20% margin may need 8x or higher to break even on new customer acquisition. The breakeven ROAS formula is: 1 divided by gross margin percentage. That's the number to watch before you decide whether a campaign is working.

### How does ROAS interact with customer lifetime value?
ROAS typically measures first-order revenue, which understates the true return on acquisition spend for brands with strong repeat purchase rates. A customer acquired at a 2x ROAS who goes on to buy five more times has generated far more value than the initial ROAS implies. This is why some brands calculate blended ROAS over a 90- or 180-day window rather than attributing only the first transaction. First-purchase ROAS is useful. But it's an incomplete picture for any brand where the second and third purchase do the heavy lifting.

## Related Terms

- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** The total cost to acquire a new customer - a closely related metric to ad spend that factors in all acquisition channels, not just paid.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLTV).** The long-run revenue per customer that puts first-purchase ROAS in proper context.
- **Product margin.** The gross margin percentage that determines what ROAS is actually needed to be profitable.
- **Gross merchandise value (GMV).** Total order volume, which ROAS calculations draw on for the revenue numerator.
