# What Is Customer Effort Score (CES)?

**Customer Effort Score (CES)** is a CX metric that measures how much effort a customer had to put into completing a specific interaction - resolving a support issue, making a return, or checking out. It's collected via a post-interaction survey, usually a single question rated on a 7-point scale from "Very Difficult" to "Very Easy."

The logic behind CES is straightforward: reducing friction is more predictive of loyalty than delighting customers. Research from Gartner found that 96% of customers who had high-effort interactions reported being disloyal afterward, compared to 9% after low-effort ones. That gap is significant. Design matters.

## Key Aspects

- **Benchmark:** On a 7-point scale, a CES of 5.5 or above is generally considered strong.
- **Key distinction:** CES measures effort at a specific interaction level, while CSAT measures overall satisfaction - a customer can be satisfied but still report high effort, which is a churn warning sign.

## FAQs

### How is CES calculated?
CES is calculated by averaging all responses to the effort question. On a 7-point scale, a CES of 5.5 or above is generally considered good. Some brands use a simpler 5-point scale and calculate CES as a percentage of easy ratings (4s and 5s) divided by total responses. The exact formula depends on the scale chosen, so it's worth standardizing internally before benchmarking externally. Comparing your 7-point CES to someone else's 5-point CES isn't useful.

### What's the difference between CES and CSAT?
CSAT measures satisfaction - how happy a customer was with an interaction overall. CES measures effort - specifically how hard it was to get something done. A customer can be satisfied but still report high effort, which is a warning sign. CES is particularly useful for support and post-purchase flows, where friction compounds and drives churn. They're complementary, not interchangeable. Run both where the stakes are high enough to warrant it.

### What is a good Customer Effort Score?
On a 7-point scale, a CES above 5.5 is considered strong. But the key isn't hitting a particular number - it's tracking direction over time. An improving CES on your returns flow or loyalty redemption process is a meaningful signal, even if the absolute score looks modest. High-effort touchpoints identified through CES surveys are typically the first candidates for UX improvements. That's where the work should go first.

### Why does CES matter for loyalty programs?
A loyalty program with high-effort redemption is a loyalty program that doesn't work. If customers find it difficult to use their points, check their tier status, or understand what they've earned, they disengage - regardless of how generous the rewards look on paper. CES is one of the fastest ways to diagnose where a loyalty experience is creating friction rather than reinforcing it. And a loyalty program that creates friction is worse than no loyalty program at all, because it actively damages the relationship it was built to strengthen.

## Related Terms

- **Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT).** Measures how happy a customer felt after an interaction - related to CES but focused on satisfaction rather than effort level.
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS).** A broader loyalty metric that captures overall brand sentiment rather than interaction-level friction.
- **Customer churn rate.** The percentage of customers who don't return. High-effort experiences are directly linked to elevated churn rates.
- **Customer journey.** The full sequence of interactions a customer has with a brand. CES pinpoints the specific steps where effort spikes.
