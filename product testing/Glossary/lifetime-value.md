# What Is Lifetime Value (LTV)?

**Lifetime value (LTV)** is the total revenue a business expects to generate from a single customer over the entire duration of their relationship with the brand. It's a forward-looking metric that shapes how much a company can rationally spend to acquire and retain customers.

LTV is often used interchangeably with CLV (customer lifetime value). Both refer to the same concept - the distinction is mostly terminological. In practice, LTV tends to appear in investor and finance contexts, while CLV is more common in marketing and retention analysis.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** LTV = average order value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan; margin-adjusted version multiplies that result by gross margin % to reflect what is actually available to offset CAC.
- **Benchmark:** A healthy LTV:CAC ratio is 3:1 or higher; top DTC brands typically operate at 4:1 to 6:1.

## FAQs

### What is lifetime value in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, LTV reflects not just what a customer has spent but what they're expected to spend across future purchases. It factors in purchase frequency, average order value, and how long the typical customer relationship lasts. A brand selling $30 supplements with a 90-day repurchase cycle has a very different LTV profile than one selling $400 furniture - even if both have the same first-order revenue. The category shapes the math completely.

### What's the difference between LTV and CLV?
LTV and CLV are the same metric. The terminology shifts by context. "LTV" is common in SaaS, fintech, and investor reporting. "CLV" or "CLTV" is more frequently used in DTC e-commerce and CRM analytics. Some teams use LTV to refer to historical, observed revenue from a cohort and CLV to refer to predicted future value - but this distinction isn't universal. When you see either term, confirm the definition being used. Don't assume.

### What is a good LTV for e-commerce?
The right LTV benchmark depends entirely on CAC and margins. What matters most isn't the absolute number - it's the LTV:CAC ratio. A commonly cited target is 3:1 or higher: for every dollar spent acquiring a customer, the business should recover three or more in lifetime revenue. E-commerce brands with LTV:CAC below 1:1 are typically losing money on customer acquisition. Top DTC brands - think Nike's direct channel or Apple's ecosystem - often operate at 4:1 to 6:1. That's the target worth building toward.

### How do you calculate LTV?
The standard LTV formula is: LTV = average order value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan. For example: a brand where the average customer spends $75 per order, buys 4 times per year, and stays active for 2 years has an LTV of $600. But a more precise version factors in gross margin: LTV = (average order value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan) x gross margin %. That margin-adjusted figure is what's actually available to offset CAC. Use that version. The non-adjusted number flatters you.

## Related Terms

- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The same metric as LTV, used more commonly in marketing and retention contexts.
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** The cost of acquiring a single new customer. LTV is only meaningful relative to CAC.
- **LTV:CAC ratio.** The ratio of lifetime value to acquisition cost. The primary indicator of whether a customer acquisition strategy is economically viable.
- **Purchase frequency.** One of the three core inputs in the LTV formula alongside average order value and customer lifespan.
