# What Is Customer Retention?

**Customer retention** is a brand's ability to keep existing customers returning and buying over time instead of defecting to competitors. It's measured by what percentage of your customer base makes a repeat purchase within a defined period - and it's one of the most direct drivers of profitability in e-commerce.

Retention is distinct from acquisition. It covers everything a brand does after the first sale: follow-up communication, loyalty programs, post-purchase experience, and customer service. Brands with high retention rates don't just sell more - they spend less to do it. That's the compounding effect that makes retention worth obsessing over.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** Retention Rate = ((Customers at End of Period - New Customers Acquired) / Customers at Start of Period) x 100.
- **Benchmark:** A 90-day repeat purchase rate above 25% is considered strong for most Shopify merchants, though benchmarks vary by category.
- **Rule of thumb:** A 5% increase in customer retention can increase profits by 25-95%, according to [Reichheld & Sasser, HBR 1990](https://hbr.org/1990/09/zero-defections-quality-comes-to-services).

## FAQs

### What is customer retention in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, customer retention refers to the rate at which shoppers return to buy again rather than converting once and disappearing. It's typically expressed as a retention rate percentage over 30, 90, or 365 days. For most Shopify merchants, a strong 90-day repeat purchase rate sits above 25% - though benchmarks vary significantly by category. Your category sets the baseline; your program determines where you land relative to it.

### What's the difference between customer retention and customer loyalty?
Retention is behavioral - it measures whether a customer comes back. Loyalty is attitudinal - it reflects whether a customer prefers your brand and would choose it over alternatives. A customer can have high retention without strong loyalty (habit, convenience, or price can explain it). Loyalty typically produces more durable, less price-sensitive retention. The distinction matters when you're deciding what problem you're actually trying to solve.

### How is customer retention rate calculated?
The standard formula is: ((customers at end of period - new customers acquired) / customers at start of period) x 100. If you started a quarter with 500 customers, acquired 100 new ones, and ended with 480, your retention rate is 76%. The metric is most useful tracked consistently over time rather than as a one-time snapshot. One number tells you nothing. A trend tells you everything.

### Does customer retention actually affect profitability?
Yes - and the math is well-documented. Returning customers typically have lower CAC (you've already paid to acquire them), higher average order value, and higher purchase frequency. That combination compounds directly into CLV. Brands that improve retention rate by even 5 percentage points tend to see disproportionate gains in margin because the revenue costs less to generate. Acquisition gets the attention; retention generates the profit.

## Related Terms

- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The total revenue expected from a single customer over their relationship with a brand - the primary metric customer retention is built to grow.
- **Churn rate.** The percentage of customers who don't return within a given period. The inverse of retention rate.
- **Repeat purchase rate.** The share of customers who make more than one purchase. A more granular signal of retention health than retention rate alone.
- **Loyalty program.** A structured rewards system designed to incentivize repeat purchases - one of the most common tools for improving customer retention.
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** The cost to acquire a new customer. High retention reduces dependence on paid acquisition and lowers effective CAC over time.
