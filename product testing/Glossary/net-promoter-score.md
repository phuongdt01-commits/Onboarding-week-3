# What Is Net Promoter Score (NPS)?

**Net Promoter Score (NPS)** is a customer loyalty metric that measures how likely customers are to recommend a brand to others, on a scale of 0 to 10. Developed by Fred Reichheld and introduced in a 2003 Harvard Business Review article, it's become one of the most widely used customer satisfaction benchmarks across industries.

Respondents are grouped into three categories: Promoters (9-10), Passives (7-8), and Detractors (0-6). NPS = % Promoters - % Detractors. Scores range from -100 to +100.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** NPS = % Promoters (9-10) minus % Detractors (0-6). Scores range from -100 to +100.
- **E-commerce benchmarks:** Above 50 is excellent; 30-50 is good; below 0 means more detractors than promoters.
- **Revenue gap:** Promoters generate significantly higher lifetime revenue than detractors, driven by higher purchase frequency and organic referrals ([Reichheld, HBR 2003](https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow)).

## FAQs

### What is NPS in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, NPS is typically collected through post-purchase surveys sent via email or SMS, often 7 to 14 days after delivery. It captures overall sentiment about the brand - not just the product. A customer who loved the product but had a frustrating checkout or slow shipping may score the brand low regardless of product quality. That breadth is what makes NPS useful: it's a signal for customer experience overall, not just product satisfaction or marketing performance.

### What's the difference between NPS and CSAT?
CSAT (customer satisfaction score) measures satisfaction with a specific interaction - a purchase, a support ticket, a delivery. NPS measures overall loyalty and willingness to recommend. CSAT is transactional and immediate. NPS is relational and cumulative. Brands often use both: CSAT to diagnose specific touchpoints that need fixing, NPS to track the overall health of the customer relationship over time. They answer different questions. Don't treat them as interchangeable.

### What is a good NPS score for e-commerce?
NPS benchmarks vary by industry. For e-commerce and retail, scores above 50 are considered excellent. Scores between 30 and 50 are good. Below 0 means more detractors than promoters - a sign of serious customer experience problems. Apple's retail NPS has historically been cited at 65-70+. Chewy is frequently referenced as a DTC leader in NPS, with scores reflecting unusually strong customer sentiment driven by personalized service. Most DTC brands should target above 40. That's the number to watch.

### How do you use NPS to improve retention?
NPS is most useful when it's segmented and acted on. Promoters are candidates for referral programs - they're already willing to recommend the brand, so give them a way to do it. Passives are at risk of churn and often respond to targeted win-back outreach. Detractors are the most valuable signal: according to Fred Reichheld's research ([The One Number You Need to Grow, Harvard Business Review, December 2003](https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow)), promoters generate significantly higher lifetime revenue than detractors - a gap driven by higher purchase frequency and organic referral behavior. Understanding why detractors scored low reveals the specific experience failures driving churn. Brands that close the loop with detractors - reaching out directly within 24-48 hours of a low score - consistently recover a meaningful portion of those relationships. Most brands don't bother. That's a missed opportunity.

## Related Terms

- **Customer satisfaction score (CSAT).** A transactional satisfaction metric that measures how customers feel about a specific interaction, as opposed to NPS's broader loyalty orientation.
- **Customer churn rate.** NPS and churn rate tend to move inversely - brands with high NPS typically see lower churn rates as loyal customers return.
- **Referral program.** Promoters (NPS 9-10) are the natural starting pool for referral programs. High NPS creates the conditions for organic referral growth.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** Promoters tend to have higher CLV than Passives or Detractors - they buy more often and refer new customers at higher rates.
