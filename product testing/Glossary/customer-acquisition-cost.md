# What Is Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)?

**Customer acquisition cost (CAC)** is the total amount a business spends to acquire a single new customer, calculated by dividing all acquisition-related costs by the number of new customers gained in the same period. It's the core input on the spending side of customer economics.

CAC includes all relevant costs: paid media, agency fees, content production, influencer partnerships, and the portion of sales and marketing salaries attributed to new customer acquisition. Attribution method matters here. Blended CAC (total marketing spend / all new customers) tells a different story than channel-level CAC, and conflating the two is one of the most common ways brands fool themselves about unit economics.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** CAC = Total Acquisition Costs / Number of New Customers Acquired in the same period.
- **Benchmark:** Target a CLV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or higher; most e-commerce businesses target a CAC payback period of 6-12 months.
- **Rule of thumb:** Blended CAC (all channels, all costs) will always be higher than single-channel CPA - using CPA as a proxy for CAC understates true acquisition economics.

## FAQs

### What is CAC in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, CAC has risen sharply over the past several years - iOS privacy changes, rising CPMs on Meta and Google, and more brands fighting for the same paid traffic slots. For many DTC brands, paid social CAC now runs $30-100+ for low-AOV products, which makes profitability on a first order difficult or flat-out impossible. That's why CLV matters so much. Brands that can't recover CAC on order one need to know exactly how many orders it takes to break even. Without that number, they're flying blind.

### What's the difference between CAC and CPA?
CPA (cost per acquisition) typically refers to the cost of a specific conversion event - often a first purchase from a single paid channel. CAC is broader: it includes all acquisition costs across all channels, divided by all new customers. CPA is a channel-level performance metric. CAC is a business-level economics metric. A brand can have a $25 CPA on Google while its true blended CAC sits at $80 once all marketing costs are factored in. That gap is where a lot of brands get into trouble.

### What is a good CAC for e-commerce?
There's no absolute benchmark - CAC is only meaningful relative to CLV and gross margin. The standard target is a CLV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or higher. A brand with a $150 CLV and a $40 CAC is in good shape. The same brand with a $140 CAC is not. Payback period - how many months to recover CAC from gross margin - is the companion metric, and it's the one most investors want to see. Most e-commerce businesses target 6-12 months.

### How do you calculate customer acquisition cost?
CAC = total acquisition costs / number of new customers acquired in the same period. If a brand spends $50,000 on paid media, agency fees, and marketing salaries in a month and acquires 500 new customers, CAC is $100. The most common error: using only paid media spend in the numerator and leaving out fixed costs like salaries and tools. That understates true CAC and makes unit economics look better than they are. Don't make that mistake.

## Related Terms

- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** The total expected revenue from a single customer. CAC is only meaningful in relation to CLV.
- **LTV:CAC ratio.** The ratio of lifetime value to acquisition cost. The primary test of whether a customer acquisition strategy is economically viable.
- **Payback period.** The time it takes to recover CAC from gross margin. A shorter payback period means faster path to profitability per customer.
- **Blended CAC.** Total marketing spend divided by total new customers, across all channels. A more accurate view of true acquisition economics than single-channel CPA.
