# What Is Net Revenue Retention (NRR)?

**Net revenue retention (NRR)** is the percentage of recurring revenue retained from an existing customer base over a defined period, accounting for both losses from churn and gains from upsells, cross-sells, or higher-value purchases. It measures whether your existing customers are growing or shrinking as a revenue source.

NRR above 100% means revenue from existing customers is growing on its own - expansion revenue outpaces losses from churn. Below 100%, the business is losing ground with its current customer base even before accounting for new acquisition.

## Key Aspects

- **Formula:** NRR = (Starting Revenue + Expansion Revenue - Churned Revenue - Contraction Revenue) / Starting Revenue x 100.
- **Benchmark:** 90-100%+ NRR is considered healthy for DTC e-commerce brands; SaaS businesses typically target 120%+ as a sign of strong expansion.
- **Decision rule:** NRR above 100% means existing customers alone are driving revenue growth - no new acquisition required to maintain a growing top line.

## FAQs

### What is net revenue retention in e-commerce?
In e-commerce, NRR captures what's actually happening with your existing customers' spending over time. It's more nuanced than simple retention rate because it factors in order value changes. A brand that retains all its customers but sees average order values fall will have an NRR below 100%. But a brand where retained customers buy more frequently - or upgrade to higher-priced products - can push NRR above 100%. That means existing customers alone are driving growth. No new acquisition required.

### What's the difference between net revenue retention and gross revenue retention?
Gross revenue retention (GRR) measures only what's kept - it excludes expansion revenue and maxes out at 100%. NRR includes both retention and expansion, so it can exceed 100%. GRR tells you how much revenue you're not losing. NRR tells you whether your existing customers are growing in value. High GRR with low NRR means you're keeping customers but not growing their spend. Both metrics together give the full picture. Neither is optional if you care about sustainable growth.

### What is a good NRR rate for e-commerce?
SaaS businesses typically target NRR of 120%+ as a sign of strong expansion. For e-commerce, the benchmark is different - 90-100%+ is considered healthy for a DTC brand with a transactional model. Subscription e-commerce brands often target 95-100%+ NRR. And NRR consistently above 100% for a non-subscription brand? That signals exceptional loyalty and customer development. Customers are spending more over time, not less. That's the number to watch.

### How do you calculate net revenue retention?
NRR = (starting revenue + expansion revenue - churned revenue - contraction revenue) / starting revenue x 100. For example: if you started a quarter with $100,000 in revenue from existing customers, gained $15,000 from upsells, and lost $10,000 from churn, your NRR is ($100,000 + $15,000 - $10,000) / $100,000 = 105%. Simple arithmetic. But what it tells you about your business is anything but simple.

## Related Terms

- **Gross revenue retention (GRR).** The percentage of revenue retained from existing customers, excluding any expansion revenue. The floor version of NRR.
- **Customer churn rate.** The rate at which customers stop buying. Churn is the primary downward pressure on NRR.
- **Customer lifetime value (CLV).** NRR and CLV are deeply connected - high NRR signals customers are growing in value over time, which directly increases CLV.
- **Expansion revenue.** Revenue generated from existing customers through upsells, cross-sells, or higher purchase frequency. The upside driver in the NRR formula.
