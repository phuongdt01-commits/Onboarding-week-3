# Week 3 Feedback Response

## Style Guide Feedback

### What I agree with and will add

I agree with all the feedback in both the AI Writing Style Guide Review and the Scale Shopify Store Final Review. Every suggestion strengthens the guide in areas where AI writing is most likely to drift.

The updates I've incorporated into `style-guide/writing-style-guide-v3(final).md`:

1. **Content-Type Modulation (Section 15):** Added detailed modulation tables for 14 content types: comparison articles, how-to guides, list posts, case studies, problem-solution articles, data-driven posts, review posts, beginner's guides, opinion posts, thought leadership, landing pages, pillar posts, newsjacking posts, and predictions posts. Each table specifies which style guide rules to dial up, dial down, or override for that format.

2. **Comparison Rule (Section 9):** Added a dedicated comparison rule requiring every comparison to answer three questions: which is better for most users, which is better for a specific edge case, and what tradeoff matters most. "It depends" is only allowed when followed by exact conditions.

3. **Factual Restraint (Section 9):** Added rules against fabricating tests, quotes, screenshots, or data. Strong stance is allowed only when evidence supports it. If evidence is incomplete, state the limit directly.

4. **Brand Lens (Section 14):** Added a section shifting from feature-listing to business-value-led writing. Recommendations tie back to ROI, conversion impact, retention impact, and buyer journey clarity.

5. **Evidence Standard (Section 9):** Added proof requirements for strong claims: firsthand testing, named source, product documentation, customer data, or real-world example. No vague authority allowed.

6. **Source Hierarchy (Section 9):** Added priority order for sources, from internal documentation down to attributed industry consensus. Internal product facts override third-party blogs.

7. **Directness Calibration (Section 1):** Added a spectrum from too-soft to too-aggressive with examples, so the writer can calibrate tone without drifting into hedging or harshness.

8. **Repetition Control (Section 3):** Added rules against restating the same point across intro, body, and conclusion. Each H2 must add a new layer.

9. **Product Mention Rules (Section 16):** Added rules for when and how to mention a product. Mentions must be earned, not inserted.

10. **Before-writing and before-submitting checklists (Section 17):** Added structured prompts for both stages of the writing process.

### Priority order for updates

1. Content-Type Modulation - most impactful because it prevents the "same voice for every format" problem
2. Comparison Rule - makes comparison articles decisively stronger
3. Factual Restraint - critical safeguard against AI overclaiming
4. Brand Lens - shifts writing from feature-listing to business value

All four are now in the style guide.

## Article Feedback

### Patterns I didn't notice until the review pointed them out

The "It isn't X. It's Y." pattern was repeating across sections without me catching it. It appeared in the intro, in the ad spend section, and in transitions between sections. Each instance felt natural in isolation, but stacked together they made the article sound like sales copy instead of a thoughtful piece. The style guide now limits this pattern to once per article (Section 2).

### What the review says about how I'm using AI

The review exposed three areas where AI was driving decisions I should have been making:

1. **Stats placement.** AI stacked data points in consecutive paragraphs because each stat individually supported the claim. But density control, knowing when to let a section breathe with logic instead of more numbers, requires editorial judgment that AI doesn't apply on its own.

2. **Section openings.** AI defaulted to principle-first openings ("Conversion fixes stop the leaking, but retention is what fills the bucket") because that's a clean, structured way to start a section. The review showed that friction-first openings ("You paid to acquire the customer once. They bought. Then they disappeared.") land harder. Choosing which approach fits each section is a judgment call I need to make before drafting, not leave to AI.

3. **Tone calibration.** Several sentences were "textbook-clean," technically correct but too polished to sound human. AI writes balanced, carefully structured sentences by default. Introducing deliberate roughness ("Paid ads can still work here. The problem is that many stores ask them to do too much") is something I need to prompt for or edit in myself.

### One thing I'll do differently in my next article

**Open every section with friction, not with a principle.** Before drafting each H2, I'll write down the specific situation or pain the reader is in at that point in the article, then start with that instead of letting AI lead with a framework or metaphor. The review showed this is where the biggest gap is between what the style guide asks for and what I'm actually producing.
