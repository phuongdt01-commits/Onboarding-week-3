# Week 4 Reflection

## What I know now that I didn't know Monday

**About Shopify apps:** The biggest friction with apps is almost never installation. Joy Loyalty installs in 3 minutes, but the real challenge is figuring out what behaviors to reward and how to structure tiers. Avada SEO has so many entry points that a merchant does not know where to start. Judge.me is the opposite: one clear job, almost no confusion. Before this week, I would have written about app setup difficulty as the main pain point. Now I know the real pain is what happens after setup, when the merchant has to make decisions the app does not guide them through.

**About writing:** I can tell the difference between content that helps someone decide and content that just fills space. After reading 5 ranking posts side by side, I noticed that the weakest ones hide behind neutral descriptions and avoid ever saying "this will not work for you." The strongest one (WP All Import's Shopify vs WooCommerce) challenged a common belief in the first sentence and took a clear position by the end. That pattern is not something I would have noticed before doing the analysis myself.

**About using AI:** The fact-check exercise changed how I see AI-generated research. The add-to-cart rate was listed as 8% when the real number is 4.6%. The loyalty spending stat was attributed to the wrong company. The CAC claim could not be verified at all. These were not wild fabrications. They were "close but wrong" numbers presented with full confidence. That is harder to catch than an obvious error, and it is exactly the kind of mistake that damages credibility if it makes it into a published article.

## My biggest writing lesson this week

Naming what something does NOT do is more valuable than listing what it does. Every weak post I analyzed avoided exclusions. Every strong post included them. My own app rewrites got noticeably better the moment I started writing "this is overkill if your store is still figuring out product-market fit" or "if your repeat purchase rate is below 10%, the issue is probably your product, not the absence of a points system." Constraints make advice feel specific and trustworthy. Vague recommendations that apply to everyone actually help no one.

## Where I'm still relying too much on Claude

Evidence and examples. My Week 3 article used hypothetical scenarios ("say a skincare store sends a how-to-use email on day 3") where it should have used named brands with real results. I criticized the exact same weakness in the Blackbelt and GemPages posts during my content analysis, then realized my own article does it too. The Zapier post used Muji and Tarte with specific, verifiable details. I need to build the habit of finding real cases myself instead of letting Claude construct plausible-sounding scenarios that look like data but are not.

I also still default to Claude for section openings more than I should. The feedback response showed that AI defaults to principle-first openings while friction-first openings land harder. I know this now, but knowing it and doing it consistently are different things.

## Where I'm getting stronger as a writer

My raw voice is becoming a deliberate choice, not just a rough draft to be replaced. The app rewrite exercise proved this. My unpolished rewrites were consistently rated as "most natural-sounding" in the comparison tables. Phrases like "the no-one-comes-back problem" and "shortcut most new stores quietly rely on" came from my draft, not Claude's polish. The style guide v4 now has explicit rules to preserve these voice markers instead of smoothing them away.

I am also getting better at structural thinking before writing. The rewrite plan for the Week 3 article is the most detailed planning document I have created. I mapped every transition pattern, every stat placement, and every section opening approach before writing a single sentence. That is a shift from earlier weeks where I would start drafting and figure out structure along the way.

## What I want to focus on in Week 5

Building a library of real, sourced examples I can use across multiple articles. The content analysis showed that named case studies (Muji, Tarte, Beardbrand) carry more weight than hypothetical scenarios. Right now I do not have a collection of these ready to pull from. I want to spend time finding 10-15 real Shopify store examples with verifiable results, organized by topic (conversion lift, retention improvement, operational efficiency), so that when I write the next article, I reach for a real case instead of constructing a hypothetical one.

I also want to practice writing full sections without Claude and only bringing it in for fact-checking and polish. The app rewrites showed my voice is strongest when I draft first. I want to extend that from 200-word descriptions to full 500-word article sections.
