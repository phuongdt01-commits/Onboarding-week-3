## My Rewrite (No AI)
Most merchants don't have a loyalty problem. They have a "no one comes back" problem, and throwing points at it usually doesn't fix anything.

Joy Loyalty positions itself as the flexible fix for that. On paper, it does everything you'd expect and then some: points, referrals, VIP tiers, even paid membership. But the real pitch isn't the features. It's control. You can design rewards, trigger them across 30+ touchpoints, and make the whole thing feel like part of your brand instead of a bolted-on widget.

A basic "earn points, get discount" setup is easy to launch, but it also gets ignored fast. Joy leans into things that actually get attention, social actions like Instagram comments or story mentions, rewards inside the cart or checkout, and a dedicated loyalty hub that doesn't feel like an afterthought.

For a store already doing consistent orders and repeat traffic, Joy gives you room to build something more than a generic points program. For a store still figuring out product-market fit, it's overkill. If you strip it down, the app isn't selling "loyalty features." It's selling the ability to design your own retention system.

## Claude-Assisted Version
Most merchants don't have a loyalty problem. They have a "no one comes back" problem, and throwing points at it won't change that.

Joy Loyalty covers the standard toolkit: points, referrals, VIP tiers, paid memberships. But the real pitch isn't the feature list, it's where and how those rewards show up. You can embed redemption directly in the cart or at checkout, reward social actions like Instagram comments or story mentions, and run the whole program through a branded loyalty hub instead of a generic widget.

A basic "earn points, get a discount" setup takes minutes to launch. The problem is that basic setups get ignored. Joy's value is in the layer above that: tiered programs that reward your best customers differently, referral flows that give both sides a reason to participate, and a visual experience that matches your store instead of looking bolted on.

Two things to know before installing. The free plan works for testing, but the features that make Joy worth using (automation, VIP tiers, advanced analytics) sit behind paid plans. And the app gives you tools, not strategy. You still need to decide what to reward, how generous to be, and what your program should accomplish. Joy won't figure that out for you.

If your store already has repeat customers and you want to turn that into a system, Joy is a strong pick. If you're still working on getting first orders, a loyalty program is the wrong priority.

## What Claude Changed
- Fixed fragment sentences: "It's control" and "It's overkill" were fragments. Combined them into complete sentences.
- Removed the repeated "It's [noun]." pattern that appeared twice.
- Replaced vague "30+ touchpoints" with specific examples: cart, checkout, Instagram comments, story mentions, branded loyalty hub.
- Cut "and then some" (cliche) from the feature introduction.
- Added pricing honesty: the best features are behind paid plans, which merchants need to know before installing.
- Added the "tools, not strategy" point from the walkthrough: the app doesn't guide you on loyalty program design, which is the biggest friction point.
- Tightened the closing: kept the "who this is for / who this isn't for" structure but cut the restated thesis ("selling the ability to design your own retention system") since the piece already proved that point.
