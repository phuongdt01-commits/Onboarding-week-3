## 1. Hook Strength — 7/10

The opening paragraph sets up the right pain ("you're getting orders but no reviews"), and the stats in paragraph two land well. However, compared to the writing skill's **Pain → Shock → Proof → Promise** pattern, the hook starts more observational than visceral. The skill recommends opening with a scenario that puts the reader *inside* a frustrating moment — something like "Picture this: 200 orders this month, 3 reviews" — but the current opener reads more like a summary statement.

**What's working:**
- The "silent majority" framing is smart and memorable
- Stats appear early (93% Podium, 270% Spiegel) — good urgency
- The promise paragraph ("Pick up what's most useful") is low-pressure and on-brand

**What's missing:**
- No "you" scenario in the very first sentence — it tells instead of showing
- The twist (paragraph 2) restates stats rather than twisting the knife with context about *why* it's getting worse

### Suggested improvement

Replace the first two sentences with a sharper scenario hook:

> "You shipped 200 orders last month. Three customers left a review. That's not unusual — unprompted review rates sit below 2% — but it means your product pages are telling a very different story than your actual customer experience."

This puts the reader in their own shoes immediately and makes the stat feel personal rather than abstract.

## 2. Accuracy Signals — 6/10

The article cites three external sources, but the sourcing has gaps that hurt credibility.

**What's working:**
- Podium (93% stat) and Spiegel Research Center (270% stat) are named
- ReviewTrackers (45% stat) is named
- The stats feel specific and believable

**Problems:**

1. **No hyperlinks to any source.** The writing skill explicitly says: "Cite real data with sources. Always link to the original study." All three stats are named but none are linked. A reader (or editor) can't verify them.

2. **The 270% Spiegel stat is repeated twice** (intro and Section 2). The second mention adds context ("stronger for higher-priced items"), but repeating the same stat makes the evidence base feel thin.

3. **The "below 2%" unprompted review rate** has no source attribution at all. It's stated as fact with no citation.

4. **The 7–14 day timing recommendation** is presented as the "sweet spot" without any supporting data or study. Where does this number come from?

5. **App pricing and ratings** (Judge.me 5.0 stars, Loox $12.99/mo, etc.) are stated without a "last verified" note. App Store data changes frequently.

### Suggested improvements

- Add hyperlinks to Podium, Spiegel, and ReviewTrackers sources
- Attribute the "below 2%" claim to a specific source or soften it to "industry estimates suggest" if no hard source exists
- Add a source or at minimum "based on our testing" for the 7–14 day timing recommendation
- Add a "Pricing verified as of [month] 2026" note to the app comparison section
- Introduce at least one additional stat in Section 2 to avoid relying on the same Spiegel number twice

## 3. Scannability — 9/10

This is the article's strongest area. The structure is clean and a skim-reader would get real value.

**What's working:**
- H2/H3 hierarchy is correct and consistent
- Headers are descriptive and benefit-driven ("5 Free Strategies to Start Collecting Reviews Today" tells you exactly what's coming)
- Numbered list for the 5 strategies makes it easy to scan
- Paragraphs are short (3–4 sentences max, sometimes 1–2)
- Bold text highlights key numbers and takeaways without overdoing it
- The "How to Choose" section uses bullets with clear audience segments
- Screenshot/example placeholders are well-placed

**Minor improvement:**
- The review app section (Judge.me, Loox, etc.) would scan better as a structured format per the writing skill's template (What it is / Standout features / Pricing / Best for) rather than single-paragraph descriptions. Even just bolding the price and best-for statement within each paragraph would help.

## 4. Actionability — 9/10

The "Your Next Steps" section is excellent — specific, time-bound, and ordered by effort level. A reader finishes this article knowing exactly what to do on day one, this week, and this month.

**What's working:**
- The 5-step timeline (Today → This week → This month → Ongoing → After 50 reviews) is immediately usable
- Each free strategy includes a concrete how-to, not just a concept
- The "How to Choose" section gives clear app recommendations per use case
- QR code inserts, subject line examples, and the discount framing all give copy-paste-ready advice

**Minor improvement:**
- The conclusion repeats the recap before the final motivational paragraph. The writing skill says "Recap the core insight in one sentence (don't repeat the intro)." The current recap ("The reason most stores lack reviews isn't product quality — it's friction, timing, and negativity bias") is essentially a restatement of the Section 3 header. Tighten this to one sentence and move straight to the flywheel insight.

## 5. Voice — 8/10

The tone is warm, direct, and reads like a knowledgeable merchant — squarely in the "smart friend who runs a store" target from the writing skill.

**What's working:**
- Consistent use of "you" and "your store" throughout
- Natural contractions ("you're," "it's," "don't," "that's")
- Casual transitions land well: "Here's the thing," "And here's something easy to overlook"
- Honest about trade-offs (doesn't trash any app, acknowledges limitations)
- The negativity bias section explains psychology without being condescending

**What could be sharper:**
- A few sentences lean slightly generic: "That gap matters" and "The benefits go deeper than just getting more sales" are functional but flat. These are opportunities for a punchier short sentence per the writing skill's rhythm rules.
- The article could use one or two more "personality moments" — a wry observation or a "sound familiar?" type aside to keep the tone from drifting toward informational-neutral in the middle sections.

## 6. SEO Basics — 7/10

The foundation is there but a few elements are missing or could be stronger.

**What's working:**
- Meta description is present, under 160 characters, and includes the target keyword naturally
- Title tag uses the primary keyword + year modifier ("2026 Guide")
- H1 matches the title (good)
- H2/H3 hierarchy is clean — no skipped heading levels
- Keywords appear naturally throughout without stuffing
- The FAQ section is missing (the writing skill recommends 5–7 conversational FAQs for long-tail capture and featured snippets)

### Suggested improvements

- **Add a FAQ section** with 5–7 questions like "Do review apps slow down my Shopify store?" or "Can I import reviews from another platform?" — these target long-tail queries and can earn featured snippets
- **Add internal links** — link to related Joy Loyalty content (loyalty apps comparison article, etc.) to build topical authority
- **Add alt text guidance** for the screenshot placeholders — when real images are added, each should have descriptive alt text with natural keyword inclusion
- **Consider adding a table of contents** at the top for jump-links, which also helps Google generate sitelinks in search results

## Priority Fixes (Scores Below 7)

### Accuracy Signals (6/10) — the only score below 7

This is the most important fix because unsourced stats erode the trust the article works hard to build. Here's the action list:

1. **Add hyperlinks** to all three named sources (Podium, Spiegel, ReviewTrackers)
2. **Attribute or soften** the "below 2%" review rate claim
3. **Source the 7–14 day timing** recommendation or frame it as "based on our testing"
4. **Add a pricing-verified date** to the app comparison section
5. **Introduce one new stat** in Section 2 to avoid repeating the Spiegel number

These are all quick fixes that don't require rewriting — just adding links and a few clarifying phrases.
