## Introduction (~100-150 words)

**Hook type:** _(pick one)_
- Problem-first: name the pain before the solution
- Reframe-first: flip the reader's assumption in the first two lines
- Constraint-first: lead with what makes this harder than people think
- Contrarian hook: call out bad advice, then present your angle
- Concede-then-reframe: acknowledge a weakness honestly, then offer context that changes how you see it

**Problem statement:**
_(What tension, pain, or misconception does this piece address? Be specific. "Most people fail at X" is too vague. "You've tried X six times and the advice you followed is the reason it never sticks" has stakes.)_

**Preview:**
_(One sentence that signals what the reader will get. Not "In this article, we'll explore..." but a direct statement of what changes for the reader after reading this.)_

## Conclusion (~100-150 words)

**Type:** _(pick one)_
- "Here's what I'd do" recommendation
- A single memorable line that reframes the whole piece
- A callback to the opening tension that closes the loop
- A forward-looking question the reader takes with them

**Final thought:**
_(Must be specific and sharp. Not a summary of what was covered. Not general advice like "start small and be consistent." Land on something the reader can act on or think about differently.)_

**CTA:**
_(What do you want the reader to do next? Keep it to one action. Place it after the closing thought, not as the closing thought.)_

---

## Pre-Draft Checklist

Before drafting from this outline, confirm:

- [ ] Every H2 heading is question-based or outcome-based
- [ ] The hook has tension or stakes, not just a problem description
- [ ] The conclusion adds something new, not a recap
- [ ] At least one section has a full evidence breakdown (data, example, or case study)
- [ ] Section depths vary (not every section is the same length)
- [ ] Evidence is lined up: specific numbers, named sources, concrete examples
- [ ] No section repeats information from another section
