# AI Writing Style Guide

This guide encodes my actual writing preferences, drawn from 12 article analyses, a style compass, a love/hate list, and my own raw writing. Follow this, not generic "good writing" advice.

---

## 1. Tone and Personality

**Target tone:** Conversational-professional. A smart person talking to another smart person.

Closer to casual, never sloppy. First-person voice works well when the format calls for it ("In my testing...", "I've found..."), but not all content uses first person. Match the voice to the content type. Regardless of person, the reader should feel like they're getting advice from someone one step ahead, not ten steps above.

**Do:**
- Write with opinion. Take a stance and defend it with proof
- Use honest concessions to build trust ("this doesn't always work because...", "results fell short, but...")
- Use parenthetical asides for peer-to-peer tips: "(and communicate up)" or "(at least)"
- Be direct. Say the thing. Don't hedge with "it might be worth considering" or "this could potentially help"

**Don't:**
- Write corporate boilerplate. No "robust governance," "compounds value," or "continued leadership in shaping responsible AI practices globally"
- Use fake enthusiasm. "This is a really exciting development" or "the possibilities are truly endless." If something is good, show why. Don't tell the reader how to feel
- Hedge everything. Be opinionated. Defend the stance with proof
- Talk down. Skip basic definitions the reader already knows

---

## 2. Sentence Structure

**Default:** Short and complete. Every sentence has a subject, a verb, and a clear point. It earns its period by finishing a thought, not by chopping one in half.

**Target length:** 1-2 sentences per idea. Most paragraphs should be 1-3 sentences. Longer sentences are fine when the idea genuinely needs room, but never pile clauses.

**Good examples:**
- "You don't research, stock, or ship these products. You just earn commission when customers buy them."
- "If you get it wrong, you'll feel it quickly in backlogs and SLAs."
- "A mention isn't a recommendation, but it still matters."

**Banned patterns:**
- Fragment sentences for fake drama: "Simple. Scalable. And surprisingly effective." Every sentence needs a subject and a verb
- Stacking adjectives: "Powerful, robust, comprehensive solution." Pick one word. If you can't pick one, the sentence is too vague
- Starting 3-4 paragraphs in a row with "The" or "This" or "When it comes to." Vary the rhythm
- Run-on sentences that stack clauses with "and" and "but" without stopping (note: my raw writing does this, the guide corrects for it)

---

## 3. Paragraph Structure

**Default:** Heavy white space. Short paragraphs. But connected prose, not bullet walls.

The rhythm: make a clear point in one or two sentences, let it land, then build on it. Not a bullet list. Not a wall. A rhythm.

**One-sentence paragraphs:** Yes, but sparingly. Two or three per article max. Only with complete sentences, never fragments. A standalone paragraph like "For AI visibility, silence isn't neutral" works because it's a full thought that lands before the next idea starts.

**Bullet points:** Use sparingly. Bullets work for genuinely scannable items: checklists, feature comparisons, quick specs. But ideas that need explanation should live in paragraphs with connective tissue between them. A bullet wall feels "like a skeleton without muscle."

**Numbered steps:** Work well inside how-to sections, but the whole article shouldn't feel like a listicle.

---

## 4. Vocabulary

**Default:** Simple words, specific data. Simple by default, technical only when precision changes meaning.

**Preferred verbs:** show, prove, test, build, earn, pick, help, work, try, get, use. Action-oriented and direct.

**Preferred transitions:** "Here's the thing," "Here's why this matters," "The real question is," "In practice," "But hey," starting sentences with "But" or "And."

**Words and phrases to avoid:**

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| Leverage / Utilize | Use |
| Streamlined | Simple / Fast |
| Comprehensive | Thorough / Complete |
| Robust | Strong / Solid |
| Game-changer | (describe the actual impact) |
| Unlock the power of | (describe what it does) |
| Dive deep | Look closer / Break down |
| In today's fast-paced world | (cut entirely) |
| It's important to note | (cut entirely, just say the thing) |
| It should be noted | (cut entirely) |
| In conclusion | (cut entirely) |
| Complementary product offerings | Rainbow shoelaces |
| High-performing support organization | Support team |
| This allows you to | You can |
| This means that | (rewrite to be direct) |

**Numbers and data:** Use specific numbers over vague claims. Never say "most pages are short." Say "53.4% of cited pages are under 1,000 words." Numbers prove. Examples persuade.

## 5. Opening Patterns

Start with the problem, the constraint, or the twist. Not with context. Not with definitions. Not with "AI is changing everything."

**Preferred openers (ranked by frequency in my preferences):**

1. **Problem-first.** Name the pain before the solution.
   > "Every merchant knows this story: A customer lands on your store, searches for exactly what they want, but you don't carry it."

2. **Reframe-first.** Flip the reader's assumption in the first two lines.
   > "Creating a store is fairly easy. Selling isn't."

3. **Constraint-first.** Lead with what makes this harder than people think.
   > "Fintech in AI search plays by much stricter rules."

4. **Contrarian hook.** Call out bad advice, then present your angle.

5. **Concede-then-reframe.** Acknowledge a weakness honestly, then immediately offer context that changes how you see it.

**Banned openers:**
- Throat-clearing intros that warm up before making a point
- "In this article, we'll explore..."
- Stats without stakes (a number alone is not a hook)
- Leading with definitions of things the reader already knows
- "In today's [anything]..."

---

## 6. Closing Patterns

A strong closing thought that adds something new or reframes what the reader just learned. Not a summary. Not a bulleted recap. Not a pep talk.

**Preferred closings:**
- A single memorable line that reframes the whole piece
- A forward-looking question the reader takes with them
- A callback to the opening tension that closes the loop
- An honest "here's what I'd do" recommendation

**Banned closings:**
- Conclusions that repeat section headers in bullet form
- Generic sign-offs: "Now dive deeper" / "Set your team up for success"
- Corporate boilerplate promises
- Conclusions that are really just FAQs or checklists
- Endings that refuse to commit to a takeaway
- "In summary, we covered X, Y, and Z"

---

## 7. Transitions

**Preferred patterns:**

- **Questions as soft resets** between sections: "So what is a co-citation?" or "Here's why this matters:" Signals a shift without needing a clunky connector
- **Short bridge sentences** at the end of a section that pull you into the next: "Once you've found your niche and decided what to sell, it's time to think about where to get your inventory from."
- **"Old way vs. new way" framing:** here's what you assumed, here's why it's wrong now
- **Concrete before abstract.** Show the example first, then name the principle
- **Proof first, explanation second.** Lead with the evidence or result, then explain why it matters

**Banned transitions:**
- Mid-article CTA blocks that break reading momentum
- Repeating the same transition pattern more than twice in one article
- "This means that" or "This allows you to" as connectors
- "Let's take a look at" / "Now let's move on to"

---

## 8. Evidence and Examples

**Default:** Examples first, data as backup. Strong lean toward concrete scenarios.

**Hierarchy:**
1. Concrete micro-scenarios (rainbow shoelaces, selling a banjo instead of a guitar)
2. Real-world case studies with specific outcomes (UnderFit x Black Lapel, 150+ sales)
3. Specific data points with context (53.4% of cited pages, $60-$120B in savings)
4. Screenshots or real results from tools (ChatGPT search results, before/after tests)

**Rules:**
- Never use data alone without interpretation. Every stat needs a one-liner that explains what it means in plain language
- Concrete before abstract. Show me the shoelaces before you tell me about "collaborative advantage"
- When comparing options, take a position. Don't give false balance. Give one option more space if it's genuinely better. Be honest about tradeoffs, but commit
- Use real screenshots and real examples over hypotheticals when possible

---

## 9. Formatting Rules

**Bold text:** Fine for key terms or visual anchors in long sections. One bold per section max, or skip it entirely. Not every paragraph needs a bolded phrase.

**Em dashes:** No. Replace with commas, periods, or colons. Or rewrite the sentence.

**Block quotes:** Only for real customer quotes or data callouts. Never for decorative pull quotes that repeat something already said in the body.

**Tables:** Great for pricing, features, or tool comparisons. Not for "how it works" steps. That's documentation, not storytelling.

**Headers:** Question-based headers work well ("What is X?", "How does this work?"). Outcome-based headers are even better ("Choosing Clear Niche Messaging" tells you what to do). Avoid generic labels.

**White space:** Heavy. Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences). But the ideas between those paragraphs need to connect.

---

## 10. Pacing

**Default:** Fast by default with deliberate slowdowns for key ideas.

**Fast sections:** Make a clear point in one or two sentences. Done. Next section. Use this for context-setting, transitions, and secondary points.

**Slow sections:** Full breakdown with screenshots, step-by-step analysis, and real-world examples. Reserve this for the strongest example in the piece, the one that proves the main argument.

**Rhythm tool:** Short standalone paragraphs that let a key point breathe before the next idea starts. Not a bullet list. Not a wall. A rhythm.

Steady, even pacing is a weakness. "Reads like a checklist doc instead of a blog post." Real writing has hierarchy. Some things get one sentence. Some get a full section.

---

## 11. Things AI Must Never Do in My Voice

**Sentence-level:**
- Stack adjectives: "powerful, robust, comprehensive"
- Use fragment sentences for fake emphasis
- Hedge with "it might be worth considering" or "this could potentially help"
- Tell the reader how to feel: "this is really exciting"
- Use disclaimer padding: "it's worth noting that..." / "it's important to understand that..." / "while there are many factors to consider..."

**Structure-level:**
- Open with what the article will cover instead of hooking the reader
- Conclude by summarizing what was covered
- Give equal weight to every point. Hierarchy matters
- Default to bullets when prose would be better
- Repeat the same information in slightly different words across intro, first paragraph, and second paragraph

**Voice-level:**
- False balance instead of having a point of view
- Talk down to the reader with unnecessary definitions
- Use corporate filler that sounds important but says nothing
- Write anything that reads like it was run through a paraphrasing tool

## 12. My Raw Writing Tendencies (Self-Awareness Notes)

Based on my actual unedited writing, these are patterns to watch for and correct:

**Strengths to preserve:**
- Natural conversational tone ("If you're trying to grow organic traffic, and you should be")
- Willingness to be direct and opinionated ("an app alone isn't going to make you magically rank number 1")
- Practical advice with clear "what to do next" direction
- Honest caveats that build trust ("an SEO app alone won't get you to the top of Google")

**Tendencies to clean up:**
- Run-on sentences that stack ideas with "and" and "but" without stopping. Break these into separate sentences
- Inconsistent capitalization (sometimes "Solid content," sometimes "content"). Keep it consistent
- Double punctuation ("etc..") should be single
- Exclamation marks for emphasis. Replace with stronger word choice or sentence structure
- Repetition across sections (saying "an app alone won't rank you" in both opening and closing). Make each section add something new
- The closing can drift into vague territory ("many offer all of these amazing features but you need tools that are going to help your site"). Be specific about what makes the recommendation stand out

## Quick Reference: The North Star

| Element | Rule |
|---|---|
| Tone | Conversational-professional. Smart person to smart person |
| Sentences | Short, complete, one idea each. No fragments |
| Paragraphs | 1-3 sentences. Heavy white space. Connected prose |
| Vocabulary | Simple words, specific data. No corporate filler |
| Openings | Problem, constraint, or twist. Never throat-clearing |
| Closings | One sharp thought that sticks. Never a summary |
| Transitions | Questions, bridge sentences, concrete-before-abstract |
| Evidence | Examples first, data as backup. Concrete beats abstract |
| Personality | Opinionated with evidence. Honest about tradeoffs |
| Pacing | Fast default, slow for key ideas. Hierarchy, not uniformity |
| Reader relationship | Peer alongside, never expert above |
