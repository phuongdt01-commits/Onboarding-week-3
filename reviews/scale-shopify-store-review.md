## Scoring Guide

Rate each criterion from 1-10. Use the benchmarks below to calibrate.

### 1. Voice Consistency

_Does the writing match the Style Guide tone? Conversational-professional, peer-to-peer, opinionated with evidence._

| Score | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 3 | Reads like corporate copy or AI output. Formal, hedging, no personality. |
| 5 | Tone is mostly right but drifts into generic phrasing in spots. Some hedging or disclaimer padding slips through. |
| 7 | Consistent conversational-professional tone throughout. Direct, opinionated, with honest concessions where needed. |
| 9 | Voice is sharp and unmistakable. Every sentence sounds like a smart person giving peer-to-peer advice. No false balance, no talking down. |

**Score:** 7/10
**Notes:** The tone holds steady throughout. Direct, opinionated, and peer-to-peer. A few spots drift slightly formulaic, particularly in the retention section where the data-point-then-interpretation rhythm repeats without variation. The piece takes clear positions (retention is the most underused lever, organic beats paid at this stage) and defends them with evidence, which is right for this voice. Doesn't quite reach a 9 because some paragraphs read more like "well-structured advice article" than "smart person talking to you."

### 2. Hook Strength

_Does the intro create tension or stakes within the first two sentences? Does it make the reader feel something, not just describe a topic?_

| Score | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 3 | Opens with throat-clearing, a definition, or "In today's..." No reason to keep reading. |
| 5 | Names a problem but without stakes. The reader understands the topic but doesn't feel pulled in. |
| 7 | Strong problem-first or reframe-first hook. The reader knows why this matters to them within two sentences. |
| 9 | The opener flips an assumption or creates real tension. The reader can't skip to the next tab. |

**Score:** 6/10
**Notes:** The opener names the problem clearly and the contrarian framing works ("that advice isn't wrong, but it skips the part that actually matters"). But the tension is moderate, not sharp. The reader understands the situation without feeling the sting of it. The second paragraph ("Three growth levers decide whether your store scales or stalls") moves into framework preview too quickly, which reads close to "In this article, we'll cover..." territory. A stronger hook would let the pain sit longer before introducing the framework.

### 3. Structure and Flow

_Does the piece progress logically? Do transitions connect sections without repeating the same pattern? Does pacing vary between sections?_

| Score | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 3 | Sections feel random or disconnected. Same transition pattern repeated throughout. Every section is the same length and weight. |
| 5 | Logical order but transitions are mechanical ("Let's look at..." / "Now let's move on to..."). Pacing is mostly uniform. |
| 7 | Clear progression with varied transitions. Key ideas get full breakdowns while secondary points stay brief. Sections connect through bridge sentences, questions, or direct pivots. |
| 9 | Structure feels inevitable. Each section builds on the last. Pacing has deliberate rhythm: fast for context, slow for the strongest evidence. Transitions are invisible. |

**Score:** 7/10
**Notes:** The three-lever framework gives the piece a clear spine, and the diagnostic section before the deep dives is a smart structural choice. Pacing varies well: the diagnostic section is short and fast, the conversion section gets the deepest treatment with two substantial H3s, and the traffic section balances prose with scannable lists. Transitions are varied (question into diagnostic section, bridge sentence into conversion, direct pivot into retention). Two issues hold this back from an 8: the first H2 ("Why Just Spend More Fails") and the introduction cover similar ground, which makes the opening feel slightly redundant. And the transition from the traffic section into the conversion section is abrupt since the traffic section ends on a closing thought about cold prospecting rather than bridging to conversion.

### 4. Accuracy

_Are claims backed by specific, named sources? Are numbers precise? Is any data presented without interpretation?_

| Score | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 3 | Vague authority throughout ("studies show," "experts agree"). Claims with no supporting evidence. |
| 5 | Some sources named, but a few vague claims remain. Data is present but occasionally missing interpretation. |
| 7 | All major claims cite specific sources. Every stat has a plain-language interpretation. No vague authority. |
| 9 | Every claim is sourced and verifiable. Data points include context that explains why they matter. Evidence hierarchy is clear: concrete examples first, data as backup. |

**Score:** 8/10
**Notes:** Every major claim cites a specific source: Baymard Institute, Littledata, Growth Suite, RckstrMedia, Elumynt, Optimove, Bain & Company, BLOY Loyalty, Artisan Growth Strategies. Stats are interpreted in plain language (e.g., the cart abandonment math showing 9-18 recovered orders at $675-$1,350). Two case studies are used effectively (NYC clothing brand, supplement brand). Loses a point because the "5-7x acquisition vs. retention cost" figure is presented without the caveat noted in the research file (the original Bain study methodology is dated, and the range is directional rather than precise). Also, one claim lacks a source: "micro-influencer with 5,000 engaged followers in your niche will drive more qualified traffic than a broad prospecting campaign" is stated as fact without evidence.

### 5. Readability

_Are sentences short and complete? Are paragraphs 1-3 sentences with heavy white space? Is connected prose used instead of bullet walls?_

| Score | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 3 | Wall-of-text paragraphs or bullet walls with no connective tissue. Run-on sentences stacking clauses with "and" and "but." Fragment sentences used for fake drama. |
| 5 | Paragraphs are a reasonable length but some run long. A few run-on sentences. Occasional bullet lists where prose would work better. |
| 7 | Short paragraphs with white space. Sentences are complete and carry one idea each. Bullets reserved for genuinely scannable items. |
| 9 | Every paragraph earns its length. Sentence rhythm varies naturally. White space is generous. Ideas live in connected prose with clear breathing room between them. |

**Score:** 6/10
**Notes:** Most paragraphs stay within 1-3 sentences and white space is generous. Bullets and numbered lists are reserved for genuinely scannable items (the product page audit, the email sequence, the traffic priorities). However, several issues lower this score. A fragment sentence appears on line 113: "Not all three at once." Two short sentences on line 15 ("The problem isn't your budget. It's that you don't know whether...") feel choppy in a way the user flagged during earlier revisions. Some paragraphs in the retention section (lines 97, 99) run long at 3-4 sentences with stacked clauses. Line 13 has two abrupt short sentences at the end ("The ads didn't change. The store did.") that border on fragment-style drama.

| 9 | Headings alone tell the story of the article. Formatting choices are invisible because they serve the reader perfectly. Every visual element earns its place. |

**Score:** 7/10
**Notes:** H2 headings are outcome-based and specific ("Why 'Just Spend More' Fails After $10K/Month," "What's Actually Killing Your Conversion Rate"). H3 headings are action-oriented and clear. Bold is used sparingly and strategically in the diagnostic section and list items. The cart abandonment email table is a good format choice for a quick comparison. No em dashes. One issue: the "Conclusion" heading is a generic label. The Style Guide says headings should be question-based or outcome-based, and "Conclusion" fails that test. It should either be cut entirely (just let the closing flow) or replaced with something specific.

## Top Strengths

1. **Evidence quality is strong.** Every major claim cites a named source, and stats are consistently followed by plain-language interpretation. The two case studies (NYC clothing brand, supplement brand) are used effectively across multiple sections without feeling repetitive.
2. **The three-lever framework gives the piece a clear spine.** The diagnostic section is a genuinely useful tool the reader can apply immediately, and it creates a natural structure for the deep-dive sections that follow.

## Priority Fixes

1. **Strengthen the hook.** The introduction moves to the framework preview too quickly. Let the pain of wasted ad spend sit longer before presenting the three-lever solution. The second paragraph reads close to "here's what this article covers," which the Style Guide bans.
2. **Fix readability issues flagged during drafting.** A fragment sentence on line 113 ("Not all three at once."), choppy sentence pairs on lines 13 and 15, and long paragraphs in the retention section (lines 97, 99) all need attention. These are the same patterns the user corrected in earlier sections.
3. **Add the missing CTA and fix the "Conclusion" heading.** The outline calls for a diagnostic checklist download or a link to a related article. The heading "Conclusion" is a generic label that violates the Style Guide's heading rules.
